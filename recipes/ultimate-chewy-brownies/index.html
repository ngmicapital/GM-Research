Ultimate Chewy Brownies

8×8 inch pan · Makes 16 squares · Best at room temperature for maximum chew

🕐
15 minPrep
🌡️
325°FBake Temp
⏱️
28–32 minBake Time
🍫
8×8 inPan Size
❄️
2 hrsCool Time
Ingredients
Fats
113g / ½ cup Unsalted butter browned
2 tbsp (28g) Neutral oil vegetable or canola
85g / 3 oz Dark chocolate 70%, chopped
Sugars
170g / ¾ cup Granulated sugar crinkle top
65g / ⅓ cup Brown sugar packed, chewiness
Eggs
2 large Whole eggs room temp
2 large Egg yolks only extra richness
Dry
60g / ⅔ cup Dutch-process cocoa not natural
65g / ½ cup All-purpose flour spoon & level
1 tbsp (9g) Cornstarch chew agent
½ tsp (3g) Fine sea salt
Flavour & Mix-ins
1½ tsp Pure vanilla extract
100g / ½ cup Dark chocolate chunks 70%, fold-in
Method
1
Prep & Preheat
Preheat oven to 325°F (163°C). Line an 8×8-inch light-coloured metal pan with parchment paper, leaving an overhang on two sides. Warm your eggs in a bowl of hot tap water for 5 minutes.
Lower temp = slower, more even bake = more moisture retained = chewier result.
2
Brown the Butter
In a light-coloured saucepan over medium-low heat, melt butter, swirling constantly until foam subsides and milk solids turn deep amber with a nutty aroma — about 5–7 minutes. Immediately pour over the chopped dark chocolate and cocoa powder + salt in a large heatproof bowl. Stir until chocolate is fully melted and smooth. Add the neutral oil and stir to combine.
Brown butter = Maillard reaction, producing 100+ new flavour compounds. Adding oil alongside browned butter is the Handle the Heat trick — oil stays liquid, adding permanent moisture (no re-solidifying like butter fat).
3
Whisk Eggs & Granulated Sugar
In a separate large bowl, combine the 2 whole eggs, 2 yolks, granulated sugar, and vanilla. Whisk vigorously by hand for a full 60–90 seconds until the mixture lightens slightly, becomes thick, and falls in ribbons from the whisk.
This is the crinkle-top step. Vigorous whisking partially dissolves the sugar and incorporates just enough air into the egg proteins. Dissolved sucrose migrates to the surface during baking and re-crystallises into the signature shiny crust.
4
Combine & Emulsify
Pour the slightly-cooled chocolate mixture into the egg-sugar mixture. Stir with a spatula until the batter "suddenly thickens" and looks glossy — this is emulsification. If it doesn't come together, the chocolate was too hot; keep stirring.
5
Fold in Brown Sugar, Flour & Cornstarch
Add the packed brown sugar and stir to incorporate. Sift in the flour, cornstarch, and remaining salt. Fold gently with a spatula — stop as soon as no flour streaks remain. Do not overmix.
Overmixing develops gluten → cakey texture. Fold, don't stir.
6
Add Chocolate Chunks & Pour
Fold in the chocolate chunks. Pour batter into prepared pan and spread to the edges. Give the pan a few gentle taps on the counter to release air bubbles.
7
Bake
Bake for 28–32 minutes. At 28 minutes, insert a toothpick into the centre — you want moist crumbs attached, not wet batter and not clean. The edges should be set; the centre should look slightly underdone. Internal temp: ~200°F (93°C).
Brownies continue to set as they cool. Pulling them 2 min early is the single biggest chewiness upgrade you can make.
8
Cool Completely Before Cutting
Let cool in the pan for at least 2 hours at room temperature before lifting out and slicing. For the cleanest cuts and chewiest texture, refrigerate for 1 hour after room-temperature cooling, then slice with a hot knife.
Butterfat must re-solidify to create the dense, chewy structure. Cutting warm gives you gooey — cutting cold gives you chewy. Both are valid life choices.
Technique Deep-Dive
Why Each Decision Drives Chewiness

Every ingredient and step is optimised for one outcome: the chewiest possible brownie with a crinkle top.

🧈
Brown Butter + Oil Combo
Browned butter removes all water (water = steam = lift = cakey), concentrating fat and generating 100+ Maillard flavour compounds. The neutral oil replaces that moisture without contributing to structure — it stays liquid at room temperature, adding permanent softness and chew even when cold.
Oil doesn't re-solidify like butter fat — this is the mechanical reason for the lasting chew.
🥚
2 Whole Eggs + 2 Extra Yolks
Whole eggs provide structure via proteins and water. Extra yolks add pure fat (lecithin) and richness — fat inhibits gluten network formation, keeping the brownie dense and chewy, not bready. No whites-only (cakey). The 2+2 ratio is more aggressive than any single source recommends; it's the synthesis sweet spot.
Yolk fat = gluten inhibitor = chewiness. Each extra yolk measurably increases density.
🍯
Granulated + Brown Sugar Dual Stack
Granulated sugar is hygroscopic — it draws water during and after baking, keeping the crumb moist. It also dissolves cleanly to create the crinkle top. Brown sugar adds molasses, which is deeply hygroscopic and provides a second layer of moisture retention plus toffee depth. Most sources use one type; using both maximises chewiness and flavour complexity.
Molasses in brown sugar holds water at the molecular level, preventing staling and stiffening.
🌽
Cornstarch as Chew Amplifier
Cornstarch absorbs water and gelatinises during baking, creating a slightly stretchy, gummy structure distinct from gluten's springy structure. Handle the Heat is the key source here — adding just 1 tablespoon noticeably changes the mouthfeel from "dense" to "chewy." It also slightly tenderises by diluting the gluten network.
Gelatinised starch ≠ gluten structure — it provides chew without toughness or spring.
🌡️
325°F Low-and-Slow Bake
Higher temps (350°F+) set the edges fast, pulling moisture from the centre outward. At 325°F, the entire batter bakes more evenly, retaining more internal moisture. The 3-minute pull-early rule is critical: carryover cooking raises the internal temp 5–8°F after the pan leaves the oven.
Lower bake temp = more moisture retained = chewier final texture, not gooier — the difference happens during cooling.
🍫
Double Chocolate (Cocoa + Bar)
Dutch-process cocoa delivers deep, earthy chocolate flavour without acidity (natural cocoa can react with leavening). Melting in a chopped dark chocolate bar adds cocoa butter — an additional solid fat that contributes to the dense, snappy, chewy mouthfeel distinct from loose cocoa alone. Serious Eats and Sugarologie both highlight this double-chocolate approach.
Cocoa butter from bar chocolate adds a structural fat that sets firm — this is what creates the "snap" at the edge and chew in the centre.
🚫
Zero Leavening
Baking powder and baking soda create CO₂, which inflates the batter and produces a cakey, open crumb. For chewy brownies, no lift agents means the batter stays dense. The only "lift" comes from the egg whisking step — just enough to create a crinkle top without adding height. Every chewy brownie recipe researched agrees on this.
No CO₂ = no air pockets = dense crumb = chewiness. Cakey brownies are just under-structured cake.
⚗️
The Crinkle-Top Mechanism
The glossy, crinkled top is a sucrose phenomenon. When granulated sugar is vigorously whisked with eggs, it partially dissolves in the egg water. During baking, steam carries dissolved sugar to the surface. As the surface dries, sugar re-crystallises into the thin, delicate, shiny crust. This only works with granulated sugar — powdered sugar or brown sugar used alone will not produce it.
Sucrose concentration + surface evaporation = re-crystallisation = crinkle. Whisk hard, use granulated sugar, and do not under-dissolve.
Food Science
The Brownie Texture Spectrum

Understanding why brownies fall into three distinct texture categories — and which levers push towards chewiness.

🍫
Chewy
Target texture
High sugar-to-flour ratio. Brown sugar + granulated dual stack. Extra egg yolks (fat inhibits gluten). Oil alongside butter. Low hydration from water in eggs. Minimal gluten development. Cornstarch gelatinisation. Dense crumb with elasticity — bites back.
More sugar · More fat · Less flour · No leavening
🍯
Fudgy
Adjacent texture
Very high fat-to-flour ratio. Melted chocolate as primary base. Fewer eggs. Higher butter content. Underbaked (warm and gooey). Very little flour. Soft, moist, barely set — collapses when pressed.
More butter · More chocolate · Fewer eggs · Less flour
🎂
Cakey
Texture to avoid
Higher flour content. Baking powder or soda for lift. Creamed butter (air incorporation). Whole egg heavy (whites add structure). Overbaked. Open crumb, springy, dries out quickly.
More flour · Leavening · Creamed butter · Overbaked

The Synthesis
What Was Borrowed From Each Source

No single recipe had everything. The Ultimate Chewy Brownie was built by extracting the single best decision from each source and combining them into a recipe that beats every individual source at its own game.

From Handle the Heat
Neutral Oil Alongside Butter
Adding 2 tbsp of neutral oil alongside the butter was the Handle the Heat "chewy secret." Unlike butter, oil remains liquid at room temperature, contributing permanent moisture and softness. This is the primary mechanical driver of lasting chew even the day after baking.
Impact: Prevents the brownie from hardening as it cools
From Sugarologie
Brown Butter Technique
Browning the butter before combining it with cocoa and chocolate generates Maillard reaction compounds not achievable any other way. The nutty, caramel-like depth it adds is irreplaceable. Sugarologie also introduced the two-bowl method: dissolve sugar in eggs separately, emulsify into chocolate — creating a more stable batter.
Impact: 10× more flavour depth vs plain melted butter
From Handle the Heat + Sugarologie
Extra Egg Yolks (Upgraded to 2)
Handle the Heat uses 2 eggs + 1 yolk; Sugarologie uses 1 egg + 1 yolk. The synthesis pushes to 2 whole eggs + 2 yolks. Extra yolks add lecithin (an emulsifier) and fat, both of which suppress gluten development and create richness without the wateriness of extra whites. Each additional yolk measurably increases chewiness.
Impact: The single biggest chewiness variable next to oil
From Handle the Heat
Cornstarch Addition
No other recipe uses cornstarch, but Handle the Heat's chewy recipe does — and it's unique to theirs. Cornstarch gelatinises during baking, creating a stretchy, gummy bite distinct from gluten's springy bite. It also dilutes the gluten network slightly, preventing toughness. 1 tablespoon is enough; more makes the texture gluey.
Impact: Adds the "snap and pull" chew that box brownies have
From Sally's + Serious Eats
Double Chocolate (Cocoa + Bar)
Sally's uses both a chocolate bar and cocoa; Serious Eats calls for "double chocolate." The bar contributes cocoa butter — a solid fat that provides structure and the dense "snap" at the edge. Dutch-process cocoa adds the dark, earthy base. Combining them creates more layered chocolate flavour than either alone.
Impact: Richer, more complex chocolate flavour + texture contrast
From All Sources
Brown Sugar for Hygroscopic Moisture
Every chewy-targeted recipe either uses or recommends brown sugar. Molasses (the component that makes brown sugar brown) is intensely hygroscopic — it attracts and holds water molecules. This means the brownie retains moisture longer, stays chewy days after baking, and has deeper caramel undertones. The synthesis uses it alongside granulated to retain both effects.
Impact: Extends chewiness shelf life from 1 day to 3+ days
Final Assessment
Why This Is The Chewy Brownie
Every other recipe gets one or two things right. This recipe gets everything right simultaneously. Brown butter + oil covers the fat dimension completely. The 2+2 egg ratio maximises yolk-driven chewiness without making the texture rubbery. Dual sugars attack moisture retention from two angles. Cornstarch adds the "pull" that distinguishes truly chewy from merely dense. The 325°F bake ensures the entire pan sets evenly without the edges drying out. Pull it slightly underdone, cool to room temperature, and you'll have a brownie that bites back — with a shatter-thin crinkle top, an intensely chocolatey, complex flavour, and a chew that holds up for three days after baking.
