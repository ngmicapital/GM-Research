🏋️ Training Volume and Intensity — Fewer Sets, Harder Effort Beats Junk Volume

"From sets 1 to five, you're getting a lot of growth. From 5 to 10, you're getting some. And then every time you add sets after that, we're not as confident that you're still getting more growth." — Dr. Mike Zourdos 02:35

The Natural Bodybuilder Benchmark

A study of 56 top drug-tested natural bodybuilders found they averaged only about 12 sets per muscle per week — and for some muscles, as few as six. This is likely less than what most recreational lifters are doing. 01:29 – 02:05

🔎 Source Verified ✅ Confirmed. The 2025 Frontiers in Sports and Active Living survey of 56 drug-tested Spanish bodybuilders (27 amateur, 29 professional) found weekly direct sets per muscle group averaged 8–17, with chest at ~12 sets/week for professionals and back at ~17. The "about 12 sets" characterisation is a reasonable central tendency.

The Diminishing Returns Curve

Zourdos and his team at Florida Atlantic University ran a meta-regression examining additional growth from additional sets. The relationship follows a curve of diminishing returns: sets 1–5 provide substantial stimulus, 5–10 provide moderate benefit, and beyond 10 the confidence that more sets drive more growth drops considerably. 02:07 – 02:45

🔎 Source Verified ✅ Confirmed. Pelland, Remmert, Robinson, Hinson & Zourdos (2025), Sports Medicine. 67 studies, 2,058 participants. Bayesian meta-regression confirmed diminishing returns. The volume required for the final increment of benefit was more than three times that required for the first.

Proximity to Failure Matters More Than Set Count

A second analysis from the Zourdos lab examined growth response to proximity to failure. Stopping 8 reps short (the intensity most gym-goers actually train at) still produces some growth. But pushing to 1–2 reps from failure nearly doubles the hypertrophic response. 02:49 – 03:10

🔎 Source Verified ✅ Confirmed. Zourdos et al. (2024), Sports Medicine (PMID: 38970765). The analysis supported a strong dose-response relationship between proximity to failure and hypertrophy.

Ethier's Two Failure Tests

Test 1: If your last rep isn't moving noticeably slower than the others, you're not pushing hard enough.
Test 2: If on your very last set you can do more reps than your first set at the same weight, you weren't close to failure. 03:19 – 03:31

💡 Key Insight Most lifters overestimate their proximity to failure. The "running on autopilot with three sets of 10" pattern is the default state — and the primary reason people plateau, not insufficient volume.

⚡ Intensity Method vs. Volume Method — Two Paths, Nearly Identical Results

"The difference is pretty small. We're talking about a few millimetres. So, whatever somebody is going to enjoy and adhere to the most, they should do that." — Dr. Mike Zourdos 05:32

The Intensity Method (Time-Efficient)

5–12 sets per muscle per week. Every set to absolute failure — "you physically cannot move the weight another inch." Chest example: 3 bench + 3 incline + 2 cable flies = 8 total for the week. Whole chest workout: ~20 minutes. Total gym time: 3–4 hours/week. 04:18 – 05:08

The Volume Method (More Manageable)

12–20 sets per muscle per week, stopping 2–3 reps short of failure. Chest example: 4 bench + 4 dips + 4 incline + 4 cable flies = 16 sets. Each set is easier, workouts are longer. 05:10 – 05:30

Ethier's Personal Hybrid

Arms and back: intensity method. Short on time: intensity method. Legs: volume method — taking leg sets to true failure is "brutal and hard to recover from." 05:45 – 06:04

🔗 Connection Intensity Method → Recovery Management. Zourdos explicitly warns about "downstream effects" — taking all sets to failure generates excessive fatigue and soreness, potentially reducing training frequency and total weekly volume. The intensity method works because it prescribes fewer total sets.
🌐 Consensus Check The equivalence of high-effort/low-volume vs. moderate-effort/high-volume is well-supported. Schoenfeld et al. (2017) and subsequent research consistently find that when effort and volume are accounted for, differences are minimal. Adherence is the primary moderator.

🔄 Training Frequency — Why Spreading Sets Across the Week Matters

"Right about 10–11 sets per session per muscle group. Over that, we aren't sure if we're really getting more growth." — Dr. Mike Zourdos 06:35

Remmert (PhD student, Zourdos lab) ran a separate meta-regression on per-session volume and found the point of undetectable outcome superiority (PUOS) at approximately 11 sets per muscle group per session. Beyond that, additional sets may not produce measurable growth. 06:22 – 06:56

🔎 Source Verified ✅ Confirmed. Remmert et al. (2025), "Is There Too Much of a Good Thing?" SportRxiv preprint. Bayesian meta-regression. PUOS for hypertrophy at ~11 fractional sets per session.

Rather than cramming 12+ sets into one workout, split them across at least two sessions per week. Zourdos claims this has "the potential to speed up your gains by up to 30%." 07:00 – 07:13

⚡ Tension Zourdos claims 30% gain acceleration from splitting volume across sessions. However, the Pelland meta-regression from the same lab found that frequency's marginal effect on hypertrophy was "compatible with negligible effects." The 30% likely refers to a best-case scenario from the per-session analysis, not a robust frequency effect.

Recommended splits: Upper/lower, push/pull/legs, full body, or Ethier's preferred 5-day UL+PPL hybrid. The exact split matters far less than choosing one you'll stick to. 07:13 – 07:28

💡 Key Insight A "perfect" 6-day split you skip twice a week loses to an "adequate" 3-day split you never miss.

🎯 Exercise Selection — Three Stages from Beginner to Advanced

"The mistake that some newbies might make, especially with this social media age, is there's a lot of exercises you could be doing, but you aren't in a position where you can efficiently learn many, many exercises." — Steve Hall 07:40

Stage 1: Beginner — Master the Fundamentals

Six core movement patterns, three times per week, can build well over 10 lbs of muscle: horizontal press, horizontal pull, vertical press, vertical pull, squat, and hip hinge. 08:04 – 08:20

Stage 2: Intermediate — Individualise and Specialise

After 2–3 years, compound movements hit a ceiling for certain muscles. The body moves weight A→B efficiently, so dominant muscles take over while lagging groups stall. Ethier's squats grew glutes/adductors but not quads (switched to hack squats); bench never grew his chest (switched to machine/cable). 08:20 – 09:28

🔗 Connection Exercise Selection → Intensity Method. Finding exercises that allow safe failure (especially machines and cables) is what makes the intensity method viable for more muscle groups. Compound free-weight movements are harder to take to absolute failure without injury risk.

Stage 3: Advanced — Double Down on Winners

Stop rotating exercises. Lock in your best responders and ride them until they stall. 09:32 – 10:57

🍽 Calorie Strategy — Body-Fat Dependent, Experience-Scaled

"There's about five times the energy in the fat tissue compared to the lean tissue roughly. And if your body believes it needs to build muscle because you're giving it a resistance training stimulus, some more body fat may be metabolized to feed that." — Dr. Eric Helms 11:18

Above 20% Body Fat (Men) / 30% (Women) — Recomposition

No surplus needed. Aim for body recomposition: lose fat while building muscle. Accept ~70–80% of the muscle gain from a surplus but with meaningful fat loss. Cap deficit at 0.5% of body weight per week (~250–500 calories below maintenance). 11:08 – 12:18

Below 20% BF — Surplus Scaled to Experience

LevelMonthly BW GainRationale
Beginner~2%Maximum growth potential; nutrition is "permissive"
Intermediate~1%Slower growth rate, smaller surplus needed
Advanced~0.5%Minimal ceiling; precision tracking required
🔎 Source Verified ✅ Confirmed with nuance. Rozenek et al. (2002) studied untrained university-age males with a ~2,000 kcal/day surplus during 8-week hypertrophy training. The surplus group gained ~1 lb/week lean mass with LBM contributing up to 100% of weight gain. However, this extreme responsiveness is specific to untrained beginners — the effect shrinks dramatically for trained individuals, which is exactly why Helms scales the surplus down.
🌐 Consensus Check Recomposition for higher-BF individuals is well-supported. Barakat et al. (2020) demonstrated simultaneous fat loss and muscle gain in untrained/overweight populations. Surplus scaling aligns with ISSN and leading sports nutrition recommendations.

🥩 Protein — Lower Than You Think, Pre-Workout Carbs Matter More

"Protein overall has a very small effect." — Dr. Mike Zourdos 14:23

Research-backed threshold: 1.6 g/kg (0.7 g/lb) is the minimum for maximum benefit. Gains still occur at 1.2 g/kg — "like hard to not hit." A 220 lb person only needs ~120 g protein/day to still make gains. 14:34 – 14:56

🔎 Source Verified ✅ Confirmed. Morton et al. (2018), British Journal of Sports Medicine, 49 studies, 1,863 participants. Breakpoint at 1.62 g/kg/day. However: 95% CI was wide (1.03–2.20), p = 0.079. The direction is correct, but "1.6 g/kg" as a hard threshold is an approximation.
🎯 Against Consensus Zourdos's framing that protein has a "very small effect" runs counter to the fitness industry's heavy emphasis on protein supplementation. While technically supported by the research (the effect size is modest), this is contrarian relative to mainstream fitness culture — and one the data actually supports.

The Bigger Mistake: Pre-Workout Fuelling

Ethier's protocol: 1.5–2 hours before: slow-digesting carbs + protein (oats, Greek yogurt, protein powder). 30 minutes before: fast-digesting carbs for immediate energy. 15:19 – 15:48

💡 Key Insight The obsession with protein intake is a misallocation of attention. Getting enough carbohydrate fuel before training would produce a more noticeable improvement in workout quality — and therefore muscle growth — than adding 30g more protein to the day.

💊 Supplements — Creatine Is the Only One Worth Discussing

"It's super cost effective. It just has a really good track record of having small but pretty consistent positive effects on muscle growth." — Dr. Eric Trexler 15:55

Creatine: Boosts lean mass by 2–3 lbs in the first 8–12 weeks. Caveat 1: Most of that is a one-time water-pull effect, not ongoing tissue growth. Caveat 2: 20–30% are non-responders. 16:11 – 16:41

Responder test: If you gain 2–3 extra reps (20–30% increase) on a weight shortly after starting creatine, it's working. 16:43 – 16:56

🔎 Source Verified ✅ Confirmed. Multiple meta-analyses: creatine + RT produces ~1.1–1.4 kg additional lean mass vs. RT alone. The 20–30% non-responder rate is well-documented (individual variation in muscle creatine uptake: 2–40 mmol/kg dry mass). Trexler's characterisation is accurate and appropriately conservative.

Bottom line: You don't need supplements to build 20+ lbs of muscle. Prisoners with no supplements and limited protein still build serious muscle — the training stimulus is what matters. 17:02 – 17:13

🔗 Connection Supplements → Protein Threshold. If protein's effect is already modest at 1.2–1.6 g/kg, then protein supplements are addressing an already-small variable. The supplement industry's revenue model relies on overstating protein's importance.

😴 Sleep — The Chemical Signal Your Muscles Need to Grow

"So, you stimulate the muscle to grow, but then don't give it the chemical signal that it needs to do so. It's eventually going to just shrink." — Dr. Andrew Specter 17:17

Why Sleep Is Non-Negotiable

The body produces more growth hormone and testosterone during quality sleep. Without post-workout recovery sleep, the body cannot rebuild. Training sleep-deprived and failing to sleep well after both limit results. 17:34 – 18:00

Three Sleep Saboteurs

1. Light contamination: Charger LEDs, smoke detectors — any ambient light. Standard: "you can't see your hand in front of your face." 18:21 – 18:49
2. Noise: Even low-level noise disrupts sleep architecture. 18:52 – 18:58
3. Temperature too high: "The body needs it to be cold to sleep." Program thermostat to drop through the night — by 2–4 AM, even colder. 19:22 – 19:35

The $15 fix: Eye mask + earplugs. Ethier uses them nightly; Specter recommends them universally. Can make 7 hours feel like 8–9. 19:00 – 19:18

The Nap Safety Net

After a bad night, a 20-minute nap partially rescues performance. A study on sprinters: 4 hours of sleep decreased sprint speed by 2–3% — the margin between first and eighth place at elite level. A 20-minute nap partially restored it. 19:37 – 20:15

🔎 Source Verified ⚠️ Partially confirmed. Short naps (20–40 min) improve cognitive measures, reaction time, and attention after sleep deprivation. Sprint studies show partial (not full) restoration of anaerobic power. The 2–3% decrement figure is consistent with literature, though the specific study could not be independently identified from the description alone.
🌐 Consensus Check Sleep-recovery relationship is strongly supported. A 2023 Sports Medicine — Open systematic review confirmed sleep interventions improve athletic performance. AASM recommends 7+ hours, with growing recognition that sleep quality metrics matter as much as duration.
📋 Protocol Cheat Sheet
  • Training volume: 5–20 sets/muscle/week (intensity method: 5–12 to failure; volume method: 12–20 at 2–3 RIR)
  • Per-session cap: ~10–11 sets per muscle group maximum
  • Frequency: Each muscle 2×/week minimum (upper/lower, PPL, or full body)
  • Failure tests: (1) Last rep moving slow? (2) More reps on last set than first at same weight? If yes to #2, push harder
  • Beginners: 6 core movements (press, pull, squat, hinge, vertical press, vertical pull) × 3 days/week
  • Intermediates: Test 2–3 exercises per muscle; track pump, soreness, joint comfort
  • Advanced: Lock in winners; rotate only when progress stalls
  • Calories (>20% BF): Recomp — deficit of 0.5% BW/week (~250–500 cal deficit)
  • Calories (<20% BF): Surplus: 2% BW/month (beginner), 1% (intermediate), 0.5% (advanced)
  • Protein: Minimum 1.6 g/kg (0.7 g/lb); gains still occur at 1.2 g/kg
  • Pre-workout (1.5–2h): Slow carbs + protein (oats + Greek yogurt + protein powder)
  • Pre-workout (30 min): Fast-digesting carbs
  • Creatine: 3–5 g/day monohydrate; test for response (2–3 extra reps = working)
  • Sleep: 7–9h; pitch dark (eye mask); earplugs; cool room dropping further by 2–4 AM
  • Bad night: 20-minute nap before training
