ACSM 2026 Position Stand: The Definitive Hypertrophy Number
Meta

Published March 17, 2026 — the American College of Sports Medicine's first resistance training update since 2009. Key findings for hypertrophy-focused PPL programming:

  • Target ~10 sets per muscle group per week for hypertrophy
  • Train all major muscle groups at least 2x/week
  • Strength work: 80% 1RM, 2–3 sets
  • Power: 30–70% 1RM, emphasise bar speed
  • Advanced techniques (failure training, drop sets, periodization complexity) not strictly necessary for general hypertrophy
  • Nontraditional methods (bands, bodyweight) produce marked benefits — no gym excuse

⚡This is a departure from the "more volume = always better" position. The evidence now supports a maintenance ceiling at ~10 sets/muscle/week for most; pushing beyond that yields diminishing returns unless programming is specifically optimized for it.

Load Independence of Hypertrophy — Confirmed Again
RCT

A Journal of Physiology (2026) study reconfirms that skeletal muscle hypertrophic response is independent of external load — 30% 1RM taken to failure produces equivalent hypertrophy to 80% 1RM taken to failure. This validates high-rep PPL sets as equally effective when pushed hard. The limiting variable is not weight — it's proximity to failure. If you're leaving too many reps in the tank on lighter sets, you're leaving muscle gains on the table.

Advanced Training Systems: Small Advantage, Big Commitment
Meta

A February 2026 meta-analysis found advanced resistance training systems (supersets, drop sets, rest-pause) produced a small but statistically significant advantage for strength, with hypertrophy effects being small and non-significant vs. traditional training. Translation for dc: the complexity of advanced techniques is not worth prioritising over base volume compliance. Get the 10 sets in before worrying about the method.

Philosophy

Source: Daily Stoic — "Are You Willing To Be Cut Off?" · 27 March 2026 (Ryan Holiday)

"They would rather be cut off from Rome than cut off from their values."

Ryan Holiday, on Helvidius, Rutilius, and Agrippinus — Daily Stoic, 27 March 2026

Holiday's meditation today uses three Stoic figures — Helvidius Priscus, Rutilius Rufus, and Agrippinus — who each chose exile over compromise. Vespasian threatened Helvidius with removal from the Senate if he kept criticising. He kept criticising. Rutilius chose exile over political capitulation. The logic is simple: the things you risk losing by standing firm (position, access, comfort) are not the things that define you. The things you would have to surrender to keep them are.

For a trader operating inside a market system that rewards short-term capitulation — chasing setups outside your methodology, holding losing trades beyond your rules, averaging down against your plan — this lands hard. Every rule violation is a small act of choosing Rome over values. The cost isn't one bad trade. It's the slow erosion of the discipline framework you built everything on.

Courage in trading is not heroic. It is sitting on hands when the setup isn't there. It is taking the stop. It is being willing to go flat and miss a move rather than violate the methodology. That is what it means to choose values over access.

Reflective question for today: What are you currently holding onto — in trading, health, or life — that you are only keeping by compromising something you said you wouldn't compromise?
