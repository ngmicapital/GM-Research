Rabbit Hole

The Day Spain Expelled
Its Own Genius

History & Civilisation 31 March 2026 · Sydney, Australia
TL;DR

534 years ago today — on this exact date — Ferdinand and Isabella signed a decree that expelled every unconverted Jew from Spain. It ended a millennium of one of the most remarkable intercultural civilisations in history, scattered an entire people across the Mediterranean, accidentally preserved medieval Spanish as a living language for five more centuries, and handed the Ottoman Empire a windfall of scholars, doctors, merchants and financiers. Spain thought it was purifying itself. It was amputating its own brain.

Section 01

The Story

By the late 10th century, Córdoba was the largest city in Western Europe — bigger than Paris, bigger than London, bigger than Rome. At its peak, it had 70 libraries, 300 public baths, and paved, lamp-lit streets at a time when the rest of Europe was largely mud and tallow. It was the capital of Al-Andalus, Muslim-ruled Spain, and it was home to one of the most improbable experiments in human coexistence ever attempted.

Jews had lived on the Iberian Peninsula for more than a thousand years — some accounts trace the community back to the Roman era. Under Muslim rule they achieved something genuinely rare: not tolerance exactly, but participation. Jewish scholars served in royal courts. Physicians like Maimonides (born in Córdoba in 1138) synthesised Aristotelian philosophy and Jewish theology in ways that would shape Western thought for centuries. Poets, grammarians, astronomers — the Iberian Jewish community was producing intellectual work that reverberated across the Mediterranean world.

Historians call this period the Convivencia — "living together." It is contested. It was not utopia: Jews held formal second-class status, violence erupted periodically, and the peace was always conditional. But by the standards of medieval Europe, where massacre and expulsion were the default, the Iberian experience was extraordinary.

Then it began to unravel. In 1391, anti-Jewish riots swept across Castile and Aragon. Thousands were killed. Tens of thousands converted to Christianity under duress — becoming conversos. Over the following century, as the Christian kingdoms reconquered the last Muslim territories, the pressure intensified.

🔍 In Plain English — Conversos

Conversos were Jews (and sometimes Muslims) who converted to Christianity, voluntarily or under coercion, to remain in Spain. The problem was that nobody could prove whether they were genuine converts or secretly still practising their old faith — which made the Inquisition very interested in them.

On 2 January 1492, the last Muslim king of Granada surrendered his keys to Ferdinand and Isabella. The Reconquista — the eight-century Christian reconquest of Spain — was complete. Spain was, for the first time, a unified Christian kingdom from the Pyrenees to the sea. What happened next took barely three months.

Tomás de Torquemada, the Grand Inquisitor, presented the monarchs with a draft decree on 20 March 1492. His argument was simple: as long as unconverted Jews remained in Spain, they would continue to influence conversos — pulling them back toward Judaism. The only solution was removal. Ferdinand and Isabella signed it on 31 March 1492 — today's date — and published it publicly in Granada.

The terms were brutal in their efficiency. Every Jew in Spain had until 31 July 1492 — four months — to convert to Christianity or leave forever. They could take their moveable belongings but not gold, silver, or currency. Property had to be sold. Debts could be collected, but payment could only come in goods, not coin. In a saturated market of hundreds of thousands of simultaneous sellers, prices collapsed. Homes worth fortunes went for almost nothing.

Modern historians estimate between 40,000 and 100,000 people chose exile over conversion. Many were robbed or murdered en route. Some paid ship captains for passage only to be thrown overboard once at sea. Those who survived dispersed across the Mediterranean: to Portugal (briefly), to North Africa, to Italy, and above all, to the Ottoman Empire — whose sultan would prove far more welcoming than the monarchs who had just expelled them.

Section 02

The Mechanism

The expulsion looks, from a distance, like pure religious fanaticism. Up close, it was also a calculated political bet — one that backfired comprehensively.

The Spanish Inquisition, established in 1478, existed primarily to police conversos. The theological problem was profound: you could force a Jew to be baptised, but you couldn't verify what they believed in private. Under Torquemada, the Inquisition became a sprawling surveillance apparatus — growing from a single tribunal in Seville to 24 Holy Offices across Spain within fifteen years. But it couldn't solve the converso problem so long as unconverted Jews remained, because family ties and community networks meant ongoing influence. The decree was, in Torquemada's framing, the surgical solution: remove the root cause.

The economic calculation was equally blind. Spain's Jewish community was disproportionately concentrated in medicine, finance, trade, and scholarship — the exact sectors that a newly unified kingdom needed to grow. Jewish physicians staffed royal courts. Jewish bankers provided the credit infrastructure that funded the Reconquista itself. Jewish merchants held trade networks stretching across North Africa and into the Ottoman East. At the precise moment Spain was launching its colonial enterprise — Columbus sailed in August 1492, the same summer as the expulsion — the kingdom discarded its most internationally connected commercial class.

Sultan Bayezid II of the Ottoman Empire grasped the opportunity immediately. According to later accounts, he reportedly said of Ferdinand: "You call him a wise king — he who impoverishes his states to enrich mine?" Bayezid dispatched the Ottoman Navy to assist the refugees, settling them primarily in Thessaloniki and İzmir. Within a generation, the Sephardic community comprised roughly 90% of all Jews living under Ottoman rule. They brought with them printing technology, textile expertise, financial networks, and medical knowledge. The Ottoman Empire thrived. Spain, over the following century, would struggle.

The deeper mechanism is what economists call a brain drain multiplied by religious certainty. When a state decides that ideological purity is worth more than productive heterodoxy, it tends to win the ideological argument in the short run and lose the economic argument over decades. Spain got its religious unity. It paid for it with a century of relative stagnation while the empires that welcomed its exiles grew rich.

Section 03
The Connections

Every time a government expels, excludes, or drives out a minority for ideological reasons — and every time a competing state or city rolls out the welcome mat for the displaced — this exact dynamic plays out. The Jewish Sephardim who landed in Ottoman cities are a 15th-century version of Silicon Valley absorbing talent that other countries made unwelcome. The brain drain calculus hasn't changed. What changes is the speed: in 1492 it took generations to show up in the data; today it shows up in the next funding round.

Section 04

Further Down the Hole

Language Ladino: Medieval Spanish Frozen in Amber

The Sephardic exiles took 15th-century Castilian Spanish with them into the Ottoman Empire — and it kept evolving in isolation. Ladino (Judaeo-Spanish) still uses sounds that vanished from modern Spanish: fijo instead of hijo, fablar instead of hablar. It's what linguists call a language fossil. Only around 200,000 speakers remain worldwide, and it's critically endangered.

Biography Bayezid II: The Sultan Who Saved the Sephardim

The Ottoman sultan who dispatched his navy to rescue the expelled Jews is an underappreciated figure in history. His motivations were partly pragmatic (skilled immigrants are valuable) and partly principled. His court reportedly mocked Ferdinand's decision as self-sabotage. What kind of empire-builder was he — and why did he make this call when Christian Europe turned the refugees away?

Ideas Maimonides and the Guide for the Perplexed

Born in Córdoba in 1138, Maimonides fled Almohad persecution as a child and ended up in Egypt as court physician to Saladin. His Guide for the Perplexed attempted to reconcile Aristotelian reason with Jewish scripture — and became one of the most contested books in medieval philosophy. Thomas Aquinas read it. It shaped Christian scholasticism. One refugee's work rewired a civilisation.

Modern Echo Spain's 2015 "Return Law" for Sephardic Descendants

In 2015 — 523 years after the expulsion — Spain passed a law allowing descendants of Sephardic Jews to claim Spanish citizenship as "compensation for shameful events." Thousands of Sephardic families from Turkey, Israel, Morocco, and Argentina applied. What does it mean to offer citizenship to the descendants of people you expelled half a millennium ago? The law expired in 2019, leaving many applicants in limbo.

Civilisation The Real Convivencia: Myth or History?

The idea of a peaceful, pluralist medieval Iberia is deeply appealing — and deeply contested. Princeton historian Mark R. Cohen calls it a "myth" promulgated as a rebuke to Christian antisemitism. Others argue the myth undersells a genuine, if imperfect, reality. The debate is about more than Spain: it's about whether plural civilisations are historically possible, or whether they always collapse under pressure.
