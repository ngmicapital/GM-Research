Rabbit Hole

The Smell That
Built a City

History 3 April 2026 · Sydney, Australia
TL;DR

In the summer of 1858, London's Thames became so saturated with human waste that the stench inside the Houses of Parliament grew unbearable. Three cholera pandemics killing over 36,000 Londoners had not moved Parliament to act. Scientists had not moved Parliament to act. Newspapers had not moved Parliament to act. But a smell in the committee rooms did — in 18 days. The legislation that resulted funded engineer Joseph Bazalgette to build 82 miles of intercepting sewers, which he deliberately doubled in diameter beyond all calculated need. That decision — made in 1858 for a city of 3 million — held for a city of 9 million, 165 years later. There is one final irony: the sewers worked. They just worked for entirely the wrong reason.

Section 01

The Story

In July 1855, the scientist Michael Faraday wrote a letter to The Times that remains one of the great unanswered warnings in British history. Travelling by boat on the Thames, he had dropped white cards into the water at every pier. Each one vanished before it sank an inch into the opaque brown fluid. "The whole of the river was an opaque pale brown fluid," he wrote. "Near the bridges the feculence rolled up in clouds so dense that they were visible at the surface. If we neglect this subject, we cannot expect to do so with impunity." Parliament read the letter and did nothing.

It was not as though no one understood the stakes. London had already endured three catastrophic cholera outbreaks — in 1832, 1848–49, and 1853–54 — killing a combined 31,000 people. By the mid-1850s, roughly 250 tons of raw sewage were being discharged into the Thames every day, partly through the ancient brick sewers that Henry VIII's engineers had built to carry rainwater, and partly because a well-intentioned 1844 reform had mandated that all new buildings connect to those sewers rather than private cesspools — a decision that simply redirected 200,000 cesspools' worth of waste into a river from which London's water companies were also drawing drinking water. Upstream intake, downstream outfall. The same river, serving both purposes simultaneously.

The Thames had been dying for years. Atlantic salmon, so abundant in medieval England that Magna Carta in 1215 made reference to their fisheries, had disappeared from the river entirely by 1820. The smell was a constant feature of life near the water. And yet London's baroque system of overlapping jurisdictions — seven separate sewer commissions, each guarding its patch — could not agree on who should pay for what, and Parliament found reasons not to resolve the question. A plan drafted by a young engineer named Joseph Bazalgette had been sitting in a drawer since at least 1856, approved in principle, but not yet funded.

Then came the summer of 1858, which was exceptionally hot. Shade temperatures hit 34–36°C; in direct sun, they reached 48°C. The river level dropped. The exposed, sewage-encrusted banks of the Thames baked in the heat. The smell became physically unbearable inside the Palace of Westminster itself — which sat directly above the river. Curtains soaked in chloride of lime were hung on the riverside windows. It made no difference. MPs fled committee rooms pressing handkerchiefs to their faces. Discussions were seriously held about relocating Parliament to Oxford, Edinburgh, or Dublin. Benjamin Disraeli, Chancellor of the Exchequer, described the Thames in the chamber as "a Stygian pool, reeking with ineffable and intolerable horrors."

On July 15, 1858, Disraeli tabled the Metropolis Local Management Amendment Bill. On August 2, 1858 — 18 days later — it was law. For comparison, Faraday's warning had been on the public record for three years. Three cholera pandemics had been and gone. Bazalgette's plans had been ready for two years. The Great Stink produced legislation in under three weeks.

What changed

Nothing changed about the facts. The same sewage, the same death toll, the same engineering plans, the same costs. What changed was that the smell physically imposed the consequences of inaction on the people with the power to act. The political distance between the problem and the decision-makers collapsed to zero. This is the mechanism of the Great Stink, and it has nothing to do with hygiene.

Ground was broken in 1859. Over the next sixteen years, Bazalgette and his team excavated 2.7 million cubic metres of earth, laid 318 million bricks, and built 82 miles of main intercepting sewers running parallel to the Thames — capturing waste before it reached the river and diverting it far downstream to new outfall points at Barking in the north and Crossness in the south. These were fed by 1,100 miles of street sewers, themselves connected to approximately 13,000 miles of local sewers. The project ran over budget — Parliament had authorized £2.5 million; the final bill in 1875 was £6.5 million — but it was completed, and it worked. The Thames slowly began to breathe again.

Section 02

The Mechanism

Bazalgette made two decisions during the design process that deserve attention, because they represent very different kinds of intelligence.

The first was a material choice. He specified Portland cement rather than standard lime mortar for the brickwork. Portland cement had barely been invented — it required precise mixing ratios and would fail catastrophically if prepared incorrectly. Bazalgette implemented rigorous on-site batch-testing procedures, the first time industrial quality control had been applied to a construction project at this scale. It was this decision, more than any other, that explains why Victorian sewer tunnels built in 1862 are still structurally sound in 2026.

The second decision was more famous. Bazalgette calculated the pipe diameters required to serve London's population and then, by his own account, doubled them. He is said to have explained: "We're only going to do this once and there's always the unforeseen." The fluid dynamics of this choice matter. Doubling pipe diameter doesn't double capacity — it increases it by roughly sixteen times, because throughput scales with the cross-sectional area. Bazalgette's system was designed for 3 million people. By the 1890s, London had 6 million. The doubled pipes held. In 2026, with 9 million Londoners, his Victorian brickwork still forms the backbone of the system, supplemented by a £4.5 billion "Super Sewer" opened in 2025 to handle the overflow — a project costing roughly 700 times as much, in inflation-adjusted terms, as Bazalgette's original work.

Now for the irony. The entire rationale for building the sewers was to eliminate miasma — foul-smelling air believed to cause disease. This was the dominant medical theory of the era. Victorian physicians were not yet convinced by the germ theory of disease; most believed that illness arose from breathing the vapors of decaying organic matter. The Thames stank, therefore the Thames was killing people. The solution was to stop it from stinking.

There was a physician — John Snow — who had been arguing since 1849 that cholera was transmitted through contaminated water, not foul air. In the autumn of 1854, he had mapped the Soho cholera outbreak street by street, identifying a single contaminated water pump on Broad Street as the source of 600 deaths in ten days. Workers at a nearby brewery who drank beer rather than pump water were unaffected. Residents of a local poorhouse with its own well were unaffected. Snow removed the pump handle on September 8, 1854. The medical establishment largely ignored him. He died in 1858 — the same summer as the Great Stink — without seeing his theory accepted.

The Wrong Theory, the Right Outcome

Bazalgette built his sewers to fight bad smells, not waterborne bacteria. He was operating on a theory that was scientifically incorrect. But by removing sewage from the Thames — the source of London's drinking water — he accidentally removed Vibrio cholerae from the water supply. The sewers eliminated cholera for reasons Bazalgette did not understand and did not intend. This is one of history's clearest examples of the right solution arrived at via the wrong mechanism.

The proof came in 1866. A cholera outbreak struck London's East End, killing 5,596 people. When the geographic data was mapped, the pattern was stark: 93% of fatalities occurred in districts not yet connected to Bazalgette's new sewer network, still drawing water from a reservoir contaminated by the old sewage outflow. Areas served by the new system were largely spared. This was the statistical evidence that finally forced the General Board of Health to accept Snow's waterborne theory — eight years after Snow's death. The sewers had proven the theory by accident.

Section 03
The Connections

The Great Stink belongs to a family of political events that share a peculiar structure: a problem that has been known and documented for years, a decision-making class that has been told repeatedly and has not acted, and then a single triggering event that is not new information but new experience — the problem arriving, physically and undeniably, in the room where decisions are made. The pattern recurs throughout infrastructure history: the 1854 Broad Street pump that moved a local authority to act in days where epidemiological arguments had failed for years; the 1966 Aberfan disaster, where a colliery spoil tip slide killed 116 children and finally forced coal board safety reforms that had been resisted for decades; the Texas power grid failure of 2021, which produced urgent legislative action after years of documented vulnerability warnings. In each case, information alone did not move the needle. Proximity did. There is a dark corollary: if the Parliament of 1858 had sat somewhere other than above the Thames, Bazalgette's plans would likely have stayed in their drawer for years more, and the death toll from cholera would have climbed further. The sewers that saved Victorian London were built not because of the 36,000 who had already died, but because MPs could not concentrate at their desks. Modern risk communication would do well to reckon with this honestly.

Section 04

Further Down the Hole

Biography John Snow and the Birth of Epidemiology

Snow mapped the 1854 Soho cholera outbreak house by house, proving waterborne transmission at a time when the medical establishment was convinced disease traveled through air. He died in 1858 without vindication. The Broad Street pump handle he had removed became a symbol — a pub near the original site is named after him. Robert Koch would finally isolate Vibrio cholerae in 1883, twenty-five years after Snow's death. His dot map of the outbreak is considered the founding document of modern epidemiology and still appears in public health textbooks.

Engineering Crossness and Abbey Mills: Cathedrals of Sewage

Because gravity alone could not shift London's waste, Bazalgette built two great pumping stations — Crossness (1865, south) and Abbey Mills (1868, north) — housing the largest steam engines then in existence. Both were decorated with elaborate cast ironwork, Gothic detailing, and painted ornament. Architectural historian Nikolaus Pevsner called Crossness "a masterpiece of engineering — a Victorian cathedral of ironwork." Both stations are now Grade I listed buildings, and Crossness is open to the public. Victorian engineers believed civic infrastructure deserved aesthetic investment; these are perhaps their strongest argument.

Ecology The Death and Resurrection of the Thames

Atlantic salmon were so abundant in the medieval Thames that Magna Carta (1215) regulated their fisheries. They had vanished entirely from the river by 1820, as industrialization and a growing city pushed pollution beyond what the river could absorb. The Natural History Museum declared the Thames "biologically dead" in the early 1950s — the nadir of a century of degradation. By the 1980s, following decades of environmental legislation building on Bazalgette's infrastructure, salmon returned. Today approximately 125 species of fish are regularly recorded in the river — one of the great ecological recoveries in post-industrial history.

Ideas The Proximate Cause Problem in Policy

Political scientists call it "agenda-setting failure" when a known problem is not acted upon despite documented harms. The Great Stink illustrates a specific failure mode: the people who bear the costs of a problem are not the people who hold the power to fix it. Victorian London's cholera deaths were concentrated among the poor, who lived closest to the sewage-soaked water supply. Parliament acted when its own members inhaled the consequences. This is not an 1858 problem. Research on regulatory capture, environmental justice, and disaster risk reduction finds the same structure repeated across contexts and centuries.

Infrastructure The Thames Tideway Tunnel and Bazalgette's Limit

Bazalgette built 57 Combined Sewer Overflows into his system as emergency relief valves — designed to trigger rarely. By the 21st century, they were overflowing around 50 times per year, discharging tens of millions of tonnes of raw sewage into the Thames. The response, a £4.5 billion "Super Sewer" — 25km long, 7m wide — began construction in 2016 and opened in 2025. It is explicitly designed as a supplement to Bazalgette's network, not a replacement. A man who died in 1891 is still the reason London's sewage system functions at all.
