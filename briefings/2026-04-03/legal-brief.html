Coinbase Receives OCC Conditional Approval for National Trust Charter — First Native Crypto Exchange to Enter Federal Banking Infrastructure as Charter Wave Hits Eleven Firms

"This charter is about bringing federal regulatory uniformity to the digital asset custody business. Federal oversight through the OCC will bring consistency and uniformity to what we do as custodians for our institutional clients." — Greg Tusar, Co-CEO for Coinbase Institutional, Coinbase Blog, April 2, 2026 [paraphrased — source truncated]

Coinbase announced on April 2, 2026 that it has received conditional approval from the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency to charter Coinbase National Trust Company, a federally regulated digital asset custodian headquartered in New York. The application was submitted in October 2025. Coinbase becomes the eleventh firm to receive conditional OCC approval or file an application in an 83-day window that began in December 2025, and the most prominent native crypto exchange in the cohort.

The charter would authorise Coinbase to conduct custody, safekeeping, and digital asset services in a fiduciary capacity, and to operate as a qualified custodian under SEC regulations. Coinbase has been explicit about its scope constraints: it will not accept retail deposits, it will not engage in fractional reserve banking, and it will continue to operate under its New York DFS BitLicense (held since 2015) until the OCC process is finalised. Conditional status means Coinbase must now complete preopening requirements — building compliance systems, hiring designated staff, passing an OCC preopening examination, and establishing governance structures — before the charter becomes operational.

Coinbase joins Circle, Ripple, BitGo, Paxos, Fidelity Digital Assets, Bridge (Stripe), Crypto.com, Protego, Morgan Stanley, Zerohash, and Payoneer in the OCC national trust bank queue. The cohort is not uniform: some hold conditional approvals, others have filed applications. The OCC issued its enabling rule — expanding non-fiduciary custody powers for national trust banks — effective April 2, 2026, the same day Coinbase announced its charter. The regulatory framework and the first high-profile application landed simultaneously.

The stablecoin dimension is material. Stablecoin revenue accounted for approximately 20% of Coinbase's Q3 2025 revenue. A finalised OCC trust charter could open a path to stablecoin issuance and tokenized securities activities under federal oversight, independent of whether the CLARITY Act resolves the stablecoin yield dispute. Coinbase CEO Brian Armstrong has stated the company could not support the CLARITY Act "in its current form" due to passive yield restrictions, but the OCC charter provides a parallel federal regulatory track that does not require legislative action.

Critically, the charter approval landed while the Bank Policy Institute — representing JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, and Citigroup — has retained outside counsel and is actively reviewing whether to file an APA lawsuit against the OCC for bypassing formal notice-and-comment rulemaking. That litigation threat, if executed, could expose every firm in the charter queue to significant operational uncertainty through injunctive relief.

📎 Historical Precedent The OCC's 2020–2021 interpretive letters permitting national banks to hold cryptocurrency assets and conduct stablecoin reserve activities were the first step in this trajectory; the 2020 OCC conditional charter for Anchorage Digital (granted 2021) remains the only prior federal banking license issued to a native crypto firm.
⚖️ Practitioner Analysis

The legal foundation here is 12 U.S.C. § 92a, which grants the OCC authority to charter national trust banks. The OCC's position is that digital asset custody falls within traditional fiduciary services. The BPI's APA challenge would argue that the OCC's decision to expand this authority to crypto-native firms without formal notice-and-comment rulemaking (specifically, without a distinct NPRM under 5 U.S.C. § 553) was procedurally deficient — effectively the same argument that sank several OCC fintech charter attempts from 2018–2021 (see Lacewell v. OCC, 2d Cir. 2021).

Who wins now: Coinbase, Circle, Ripple, and Paxos gain federal regulatory infrastructure that institutional allocators require — pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, and prime brokers can now engage with a federally chartered custodian. Who loses: State-chartered custodians (NYDFS Trust Companies, state banks) face competitive erosion as institutions prefer the OCC's federal imprimatur. Traditional prime brokers (State Street, BNY Mellon) face compression on custody fees. Enforcement trajectory: If BPI litigates and wins a preliminary injunction, every conditional approval could be frozen while the OCC re-runs the rulemaking — a 12–18 month disruption. If BPI does not litigate, the charter wave accelerates and the OCC becomes the de facto crypto banking regulator for institutional custody.

🔄 Contrarian Take The conditional approval is not a licence to operate — it is a to-do list. Coinbase must pass an OCC preopening examination, demonstrate full AML/KYC buildout, and establish robust governance before a dollar of institutional custody flows under the federal charter. Given that Coinbase already operates under NYDFS oversight, the marginal regulatory benefit may be primarily reputational and institutional-marketing oriented rather than operationally transformative. The more important question is whether a federal charter accelerates stablecoin issuance — and on that front, the CLARITY Act yield fight remains unresolved.
🏗️ Industry Implications

Coinbase gains the ability to pitch institutional mandates that require a federally chartered custodian — pension funds, certain sovereign wealth vehicles, and regulated funds unable to use state-chartered entities. Fidelity Digital Assets and BNY Mellon's crypto custody division face direct competition on institutional RFPs. Circle and Paxos, already conditional OCC approvals, now have company in the federal custody tier. State-chartered trust companies — including BitGo's South Dakota charter — face reduced competitive positioning for institutional mandates. Capital flows to OCC-chartered custodians from institutional allocators currently holding assets in offshore or state-regulated structures.

Bearish: Traditional prime brokers with crypto custody ambitions (State Street Digital, BNY Mellon Digital Assets) — margin compression. Neutral: Bitcoin, Ethereum spot prices — charter approval is infrastructure, not capital inflow catalyst in the short term.

Treasury Drops First GENIUS Act Implementation Rule — 60-Day Comment Window Opens on Who Controls Stablecoin Oversight for Sub-$10 Billion Issuers

"Payment stablecoin issuers with a consolidated total outstanding issuance of not more than $10 billion can opt to be regulated under a state-level regulatory regime, if that regime is substantially similar to the federal regulatory framework." — U.S. Treasury NPRM, April 1, 2026 [direct statutory language, multiple sources corroborated]

The U.S. Department of the Treasury published its first Notice of Proposed Rulemaking under the GENIUS Act on April 1, 2026, opening a 60-day public comment period. The rule is narrow but constitutionally significant: it establishes the principles by which Treasury will determine whether a state stablecoin regime qualifies as "substantially similar" to the federal framework — the gating question for whether smaller issuers can remain under state oversight rather than federal supervision.

The threshold is $10 billion in consolidated outstanding issuance. Issuers above that threshold are mandated federal supervision; those below can opt for a qualifying state regime. Treasury's ANPRM in September 2025 drew 333 comments from state regulators, financial institutions, and payments firms. That response informed this draft, which provides states "wide latitude" to deviate from federal minimum standards while still being classified as substantially similar — deliberately avoiding a rigid checklist to retain Treasury discretion on a case-by-case basis.

The mandatory floor is clear: 1:1 reserve backing in cash or high-quality liquid assets, monthly reserve disclosures, full AML/sanctions compliance, and no rehypothecation. States can layer on stricter requirements — higher liquidity buffers, enhanced reserve audits, additional enforcement procedures — but cannot fall below these federal minimums. The proposed rule is deliberately silent on the stablecoin yield question, deferring that issue to the CLARITY Act process.

This Treasury rule is distinct from the OCC's February 2026 NPRM on payment stablecoin issuance standards (comment deadline: May 1, 2026). Treasury controls the state equivalence determination; OCC controls the federally chartered issuance requirements. The two rulemakings operate in parallel and together define the full regulatory perimeter of the GENIUS Act framework.

📎 Historical Precedent The "substantially similar" equivalence model mirrors the state MSB opt-in under FinCEN's Bank Secrecy Act framework and the OCC's 2022 CRA reform — both allowed state flexibility while preserving federal floor standards. The closest analogue is the state money transmission licensing patchwork that allowed PayPal to expand in the early 2000s.
⚖️ Practitioner Analysis

The "wide latitude" framing is deliberately pro-state and pro-innovation, but it embeds Treasury as the de facto arbiter of which state regimes pass muster — a significant regulatory power concentration. Treasury will effectively set the floor for stablecoin oversight across all 50 states through this single equivalence determination process. The 60-day comment period (closing approximately June 1, 2026) is the last meaningful private-sector input opportunity before rules finalise. Who wins: Smaller fintechs and startups issuing below the $10 billion threshold in states with flexible regimes (Wyoming, South Dakota). Circle and Paxos — already GENIUS Act compliant at federal level, but can now white-label state-compliant products for sub-threshold markets. Who loses: State regulators in jurisdictions whose existing MSB/money transmission frameworks are deemed not substantially similar — they face a forced rebuild of their licensing architecture or loss of stablecoin jurisdiction. New York's BitLicense regime faces particular scrutiny given its prescriptive requirements.

🔄 Contrarian Take The "wide latitude" standard, while politically attractive, is a recipe for regulatory arbitrage. If Treasury grants equivalence to Wyoming's permissive regime and denies it to New York's stricter framework, the result is a race to the bottom in state stablecoin oversight. The $10 billion threshold is also low enough that major issuers can structure around it through SPV fragmentation — a compliance engineering problem the rule does not address.
🏗️ Industry Implications

Circle benefits most — it is already federally compliant and can now market state-compliant white-label products using the Treasury equivalence standard. Stripe (via Bridge) and Paxos gain a clearer regulatory path for their state-chartered stablecoin operations. Community banks and credit unions in states with simple stablecoin laws gain a federal safety stamp for their digital dollar products. New York DFS faces an implicit audit of whether its BitLicense regime satisfies the federal floor — with significant consequences for its position as the premier U.S. crypto regulatory gateway.

📊 Portfolio Impact Bullish: Circle, Paxos — state equivalence creates new distribution channels below the $10B threshold; Wyoming and South Dakota state-chartered digital bank stocks. Bearish: Tether (USDT) — foreign-issued stablecoin cannot qualify for federal or state equivalence under GENIUS Act, creating structural disadvantage in U.S. market. Neutral: Coinbase USDC distribution — USDC is already GENIUS-compliant; state equivalence is additive not transformative for Circle/Coinbase's core relationship.

UPDATE: Coinbase CLO Says CLARITY Act Stablecoin Deal Is "48 Hours Away" — Senate Markup Window Narrows as Banking Lobby Holds Line on Yield Ban

The core dispute has been between Senators Thom Tillis (R-NC) and Angela Alsobrooks (D-MD), who reached a bipartisan compromise on March 23 banning passive yield on stablecoins while permitting activity-based rewards and incentives. Coinbase rejected that compromise as overly broad, arguing it would restrict legitimate customer incentive structures. The American Bankers Association has separately demanded the compromise go further — prohibiting all yield-adjacent arrangements — based on concerns about deposit substitution.

The stakes are high for Coinbase specifically: stablecoin revenue represents approximately 20% of its quarterly revenue, and any yield-sharing arrangement with USDC holders would depend on what the statute ultimately permits. A four-way deadlock persists: crypto firms (Coinbase, Stripe) want permissive yield; banks (ABA, BPI) want a near-total ban; bipartisan senators want a middle path; and minority Democrats want additional consumer protections. Polymarket odds for 2026 CLARITY Act passage stand at approximately 51%, down from 70–90% peaks earlier this year.

If markup does not advance the bill from the Senate Banking Committee before May, its window to reach the Senate floor ahead of the August congressional recess narrows dramatically — and any delay past summer risks the bill dying ahead of November midterms.

BPI Retains Outside Counsel, Weighs APA Lawsuit Against OCC Over Crypto Trust Charter Wave — JPMorgan and Goldman-Backed Group Claims "Franken-Charter" Bypassed Public Notice

"The OCC bypassed formal rulemaking under the Administrative Procedure Act, effectively redefining the scope of banking through interpretive guidance, without requisite public notice and comment." — Ankita Dhawan, Web3 Policy Advisor [quoted in CoinTelegraph, March 2026]

The Bank Policy Institute — a lobbying group whose board includes the CEOs of JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs, and Bank of America — has retained outside counsel and is actively reviewing whether to file a federal lawsuit against the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency over its decision to grant national trust bank charters to crypto and fintech firms without conducting formal APA notice-and-comment rulemaking. The BPI has not made a final decision, but the retention of outside counsel indicates the threat is beyond rhetorical posturing.

The core legal theory is Administrative Procedure Act violation: the OCC expanded the scope of national trust bank charters to cover digital asset custody activities through interpretive guidance rather than a formal NPRM, depriving the public of the notice-and-comment rights guaranteed by 5 U.S.C. § 553. The Conference of State Banking Supervisors has separately characterised the structure as a "Franken-charter" — arguing the OCC is combining authorities under different statutory provisions in ways that may not survive judicial review.

The timing is maximally hostile to the OCC. Coinbase's conditional approval on April 2 — the most prominent name yet in the charter queue — raises the political salience of the BPI's challenge. Eleven firms have now received approvals or filed applications in 83 days. If BPI files and obtains a preliminary injunction, every firm in that queue faces operational freeze pending resolution — a 12–18 month disruption at minimum given federal court timelines.

The legal precedent BPI would cite is devastating for the OCC's position: Lacewell v. OCC (2d Cir. 2021) struck down the OCC's attempt to issue a fintech charter to non-depository institutions on the grounds that the term "carrying on the business of banking" in 12 U.S.C. § 27 required deposit-taking. The trust bank path differs from that case's statutory ground, but the broader administrative law principle — that novel expansions of OCC jurisdiction require formal rulemaking — applies directly.

📎 Historical Precedent Lacewell v. OCC, 2d Cir. 2021: struck down the OCC's special purpose national bank charter for fintech non-depositories. CSBS v. OCC, D.D.C. 2019: CSBS challenged OCC fintech charter on similar APA grounds. Both cases signal that OCC unilateral expansion of charter categories without rulemaking faces significant judicial resistance.
⚖️ Practitioner Analysis

The BPI's strongest APA argument is that the OCC's March 2026 rule amendment — effective April 2, 2026 — was itself a formal rulemaking, but that it enabled a category of chartering activity (crypto-native exchange custody) that was not the stated subject of the rulemaking's notice. The notice-and-comment period for the amendment did not specifically flag Coinbase-type applications as within scope, creating an argument that the public was denied meaningful comment on the operative expansion. Who wins if BPI litigates successfully: Traditional banks — the competitive moat around federally chartered banking services is preserved. State banking supervisors — state trust company regimes retain relevance. Who loses: Every firm in the OCC charter queue; the OCC's credibility as a pro-innovation regulator; institutional clients who have already contracted around conditional charter status.

🔄 Contrarian Take BPI may be bluffing. The political dynamics of suing to block crypto firms from accessing federal banking infrastructure — while the Trump administration has made crypto-friendly banking a stated policy priority — create significant reputational risk for JPMorgan and Goldman. A lawsuit filed in this environment reads as incumbent banks using regulatory machinery to block competition, exactly the narrative the crypto industry has spent years constructing. BPI may announce a formal comment letter and stand down from litigation.

Zerohash and Bridge (Stripe) — smaller firms in the queue face operational existential risk from a prolonged freeze. Colorado AI Act Hits 89-Day Countdown to June 30 Enforcement — High-Risk AI Obligations Land Without Federal Preemption Shield as White House Task Force Targets State Laws

"Colorado is pumping the brakes on its first-of-its-kind AI regulation to find a practical path forward — lawmakers have delayed but have not changed the substance of the obligations." — University of Denver, reporting on Colorado legislative session, 2025 [paraphrased]

The Colorado Artificial Intelligence Act — the first high-risk AI regulatory law in the United States — enters its 89-day final stretch before its June 30, 2026 enforcement date. Colorado Governor Jared Polis signed Senate Bill 25B-004 in August 2025, extending the original February 2026 deadline to June 30, 2026, but leaving all substantive obligations intact. Attorney General Phil Weiser must issue implementing rules before the deadline. Draft guidance is expected imminently.

The Act imposes risk management obligations on both AI developers and deployers: developers must exercise "reasonable care" to prevent algorithmic discrimination, maintain technical documentation, and issue regulator and deployer notices; deployers must adopt formal risk-management policies, conduct initial and annual impact assessments, issue pre-decision and adverse-decision consumer notices, and publish website disclosures. Violations trigger AG enforcement and the Act's rebuttable presumptions become live.

The critical complication is the White House. The December 2025 executive order on AI governance — covered in Thursday's Brief — directed the Department of Commerce to identify state AI laws that conflict with federal objectives, and established an AI Litigation Task Force authorised to challenge non-conforming state laws in court. Colorado's Act is a primary target. If the federal government files litigation before June 30, the enforcement window becomes a constitutional battleground rather than a compliance event.

For crypto-adjacent AI businesses — algorithmic trading systems, DeFi risk models, automated lending underwriting — the Act's definition of "consequential decisions" (financial services, credit, housing) captures many standard AI deployments. Colorado-based or Colorado-serving AI systems must have impact assessments ready by June 30 or face AG enforcement regardless of whether federal litigation ultimately succeeds.

📎 Historical Precedent California's CCPA (2018) was similarly threatened with federal preemption claims; those threats were not executed before enforcement began. Illinois BIPA survived preemption challenges and became the most litigated privacy statute in U.S. history. Colorado AI Act's fate likely follows the same pattern — enforcement begins, litigation follows, outcome uncertain for years.
⚖️ Practitioner Analysis

The federal preemption argument is not settled law — there is no federal AI regulation that explicitly preempts state laws. The White House's litigation authority relies on a novel theory that the Supremacy Clause bars state laws that "obstruct" federal AI policy priorities, even absent a specific federal statute. That theory has not been tested. Who wins now: Colorado residents — algorithmic discrimination protections take effect June 30 regardless of federal posturing. Colorado AG — enforcement jurisdiction is live. Who loses: AI deployers in financial services operating in Colorado who now face a compliance build regardless of whether the federal government ultimately preempts the law. The series of consultation papers — CP25/40 (trading platforms, intermediaries, staking, DeFi), CP25/41 (admissions, disclosures, market abuse), CP25/42 (prudential requirements), CP26/4 (Consumer Duty, COBS, SM&CR), and the stablecoin-specific CP25/14 — closed in late 2025 and early 2026. Responses are under review. The FCA's CP26/8 stablecoin backing consultation (the subject of yesterday's brief note) closes April 13, 2026 — 10 days away.

The UK's framework is the most comprehensive crypto licensing regime outside the EU's MiCA. Key requirements will include: FCA authorization for all regulated cryptoasset activities (trading platforms, intermediaries, staking-as-a-service, DeFi arrangements); Consumer Duty obligations applied to retail-facing crypto products; SM&CR accountability requirements for senior management; prudential requirements calibrated to the risk profile of different cryptoasset activities; and a new market abuse regime specific to crypto assets.

The UK's timeline creates a convergence point with MiCA in mid-2027 — both regimes will be substantially operational within six months of each other. For firms operating across UK and EU, dual authorisation is now a strategic necessity. The divergence between MiCA's harmonised EU approach and the UK's FCA-specific framework creates a dual-compliance cost that smaller operators will struggle to absorb.

📎 Historical Precedent The UK's FCA authorization gateway for e-money institutions (2018–2019, Payment Services Regulations 2017) was the most recent comparable licensing sprint — it took 18 months from gateway opening to the end of the transition window, with approximately 40% of firms failing to obtain authorization before their temporary permissions expired.
⚖️ Practitioner Analysis

September 30, 2026 is five months away. The FCA has not yet published final rules — they will arrive in Q2 or Q3 2026 based on the consultation timelines. This means firms have approximately four to six months after final rules to build their authorization applications. The FCA's track record on crypto authorization decisions is slow: the current AML registration regime has taken 6–12 months per application. Full authorization under the new regime will be more demanding. Who wins: Large crypto exchanges already operating under FCA AML registration (Coinbase UK, Kraken UK, Revolut) — they have existing regulatory relationships and compliance infrastructure. Law firms with FCA fintech authorization practices. Who loses: Smaller operators and new entrants who cannot afford the compliance overhead; DeFi protocols with no clear principal who will own the authorization application.

🏗️ Industry Implications
