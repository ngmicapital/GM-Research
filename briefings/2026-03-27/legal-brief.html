UPDATE: CLARITY Act Yield Ban Wipes $10B in Market Cap — Circle Posts Worst Day on Record, Coinbase Breaks Ranks

"I'd rather have no bill than a bad bill. This language is materially worse than the current status quo." — Brian Armstrong, CEO, Coinbase (paraphrased from January 2026 statements; reiterated in March 2026 Senate staff communications)

The CLARITY Act's stablecoin yield provisions detonated across crypto markets on March 24–25. Circle (CRCL) posted its worst single session since its NYSE debut, shedding approximately 20% and erasing $5.6 billion in market capitalisation. Coinbase (COIN) fell 8%, losing a further ~$5 billion. The trigger: leaked draft text from the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act prohibiting digital asset service providers — including exchanges, brokers, and affiliated entities — from offering yield directly or indirectly on stablecoin balances, or in any manner "economically or functionally equivalent to bank interest."

The language is more sweeping than the version that Senators described as "99% resolved" earlier this month (covered in yesterday's brief). The draft explicitly closes affiliate workarounds and structural equivalence arrangements. Activity-based rewards remain permitted — loyalty programmes, subscriptions, transactions, payments, and platform use — provided they do not meet the economic equivalence standard. The SEC, CFTC, and US Treasury have 12 months from enactment to define permissible rewards and draft anti-evasion rules, leaving a yearlong compliance gap.

On March 25, Coinbase informed Senate Banking Committee staff it "cannot support the current version of the bill," citing the yield provisions as the primary concern. Armstrong has characterised USDC rewards as "revenue sharing from the interest earned on Treasury bills held in USDC's reserve" — a structure he argues is fundamentally different from a bank savings account paying interest. Coinbase reported $1.35 billion in stablecoin revenue in 2025, representing approximately 20% of Q3 2025 total revenue. The yield ban strikes directly at that income line. The Senate Banking Committee markup is targeted for April 2026; without Coinbase's support, whip counts become uncertain.

Analysts are divided on Circle's long-term position. Some argue the yield ban eliminates a key competitive advantage Coinbase held over Circle by being able to share USDC yield with customers, levelling the issuer-exchange relationship and improving Circle's renegotiation leverage when its USDC revenue-sharing agreement with Coinbase expires. Others read the 20% stock drop as the market pricing in a scenario where the bill either passes with the ban — eliminating a key retail stablecoin incentive — or stalls entirely, leaving market structure legislation unresolved through 2026.

📎 Historical Precedent: The Durbin Amendment (Dodd-Frank, 2010) — capping debit card interchange fees — passed over the explicit objection of card networks and major banks. The concession to the banking lobby cost the financial industry ~$6B/year in revenue. The CLARITY Act yield ban follows the same structural logic: regulators and banking incumbents demand a "level playing field" with banks, at the expense of the crypto-native business models that lobbied for the legislation in the first place.
⚖️ Practitioner Analysis

Regulatory trajectory: The yield ban is the American Bankers Association's price for not opposing the CLARITY Act outright. The ABA's argument — that crypto exchanges offering stablecoin interest compete with bank savings accounts without being subject to banking regulation — has traction with enough Senate Republicans to matter. The 12-month rulemaking window, while designed as a compromise, functions as a delayed liability: the language that survives the SEC/CFTC/Treasury rulemaking will be drafted under political pressure from both sides and may produce inconsistent definitions across three agencies.

Who wins: Traditional banks (level playing field argument satisfied); state-licensed stablecoin issuers not affiliated with exchanges (Circle without the Coinbase relationship); OCC-supervised national trust stablecoin banks (no prohibition on bank interest, only on DASP yield programs). Who loses: Coinbase (USDC yield program directly hit; ~20% of Q3 2025 revenue at risk); DeFi protocols offering yield on stablecoins (the "economically equivalent" anti-evasion sweep may reach on-chain yield mechanisms); retail users who relied on stablecoin rewards as a crypto-native savings product.

Enforcement risk: The 12-month rulemaking gap creates a window where yield programs exist in regulatory limbo. If the bill passes with this language, any exchange continuing yield programs before the rule is final faces potential liability under the statutory prohibition. If the bill stalls, the GENIUS Act NPRM (OCC comment period closes May 1) may impose its own yield restrictions on OCC-supervised issuers — creating a partial ban without a corresponding statutory fix on the exchange side.

🔄 Contrarian Take

The 20% single-session drop overreacts to an ambiguous draft. The bill does not pass the Senate Banking Committee until April at the earliest, floor vote uncertain, and conference with the House further softens provisions — exactly as the Dodd-Frank Volcker Rule was softened over its three-year implementation process. More importantly, the 12-month rulemaking window gives the SEC, CFTC, and Treasury to write rules that functionally permit the activity-based rewards programmes Coinbase actually offers. Armstrong's public opposition may be strategic — positioning Coinbase as the hardest-line negotiator to extract maximum concessions before eventually supporting the bill.

Short-term: the $5.6B market cap loss is disproportionate to the actual cash flow impact, suggesting the market is pricing in bill failure rather than bill passage.

Coinbase (COIN): $1.35B in 2025 stablecoin revenue is the direct exposure. The deeper risk is strategic: USDC rewards were Coinbase's primary tool for growing USDC market share against Tether. Without yield, USDC's competitive advantage narrows to regulatory trust — a narrower moat against USDT outside the US and PYUSD domestically.

DeFi protocols (Uniswap, Aave, Curve): The anti-evasion language targeting structures "economically equivalent to interest" may reach on-chain yield mechanisms. Watch for the 12-month rulemaking to produce guidance specifically on DeFi yield — the most legally contested terrain in the bill.

Tokenization Hearing: Congress Declares It Inevitable — Then Identifies Six Reasons It Can't Happen Yet

"Tokenized securities are still securities — existing SEC tools can support progress through exemptive relief. But the framework required to bring this to scale does not yet exist." — Summer Mersinger, CEO, Blockchain Association (testimony before House Financial Services Committee, March 25, 2026)

The House Financial Services Committee's March 25 hearing — "Tokenization and the Future of Securities: Modernizing Our Capital Markets" — ran five hours and produced bipartisan agreement on one proposition: tokenized real-world assets are not a hypothetical future condition. They are a $26.58 billion present reality, growing at approximately 5–6% monthly, with BlackRock, JPMorgan, Franklin Templeton, and Circle having deployed institutional-grade tokenized products. No legislation resulted from the hearing.

What the hearing did produce was the most detailed congressional articulation to date of why the regulatory framework cannot accommodate tokenized securities at scale. Salman Banaei, General Counsel of Kimber Labs, identified six structural barriers: (1) the TEFRA (Tax Equity and Fiscal Responsibility Act, 1982) inadvertently bans tokenized bonds on permissionless blockchains — penalties include denial of interest deductions, excise taxes at issuance, and 30% withholding; (2) the Howey Test was not designed for instruments that function simultaneously as securities and payment rails; (3) Basel III capital rules impose 1,250% risk weights on public-chain assets, making institutional custody economically prohibitive; (4) 66% of institutional investors cite regulatory uncertainty as the primary reason they are not deploying into digital assets (EY-Parthenon/Coinbase, January 2026); (5) liquidity fragmentation creates 1–3% pricing gaps across chains; and (6) T+1 settlement rules conflict with blockchain settlement finality.

Kenneth Bentsen Jr. (SIFMA President) represented the institutional infrastructure perspective: broker-dealers and asset managers need statutory clarity before they can build operational capacity to absorb tokenized securities at scale. Mersinger (Blockchain Association) argued for an SEC exemptive relief pathway as a bridging mechanism, rather than waiting for legislation. The CLARITY Act's April markup timeline is now the primary legislative vehicle — the hearing functioned largely as a pre-markup evidence record.

📎 Historical Precedent: TEFRA (1982) — the restriction was originally designed to prevent tax evasion on bearer bonds. Congress has not updated TEFRA for digital bearer instruments. The CLARITY Act is expected to include a TEFRA carve-out for tokenized bonds on registered distributed ledger systems, but the precise language is unresolved.
⚖️ Practitioner Analysis

Regulatory trajectory: The hearing is a threshold event — it legitimises tokenization as a congressional priority rather than a regulatory novelty. But the six structural barriers identified are not rhetorical; they are hard law problems requiring either CLARITY Act statutory fixes or SEC/CFTC exemptive relief across at least four separate legal regimes (securities law, tax law, bank capital rules, settlement finality). The SEC exemptive relief pathway Mersinger proposed could move faster than legislation but would produce patchwork solutions that the next administration could rescind.

Who wins: BlackRock (BUIDL), Franklin Templeton (FOBXX), JPMorgan (JPMCoin), and Circle — institutions with existing tokenized products are now operating with implicit congressional endorsement. Law firms advising on TEFRA carve-outs and tokenized securities exemptive relief applications have immediate mandates. Who loses: Any tokenized securities project depending on permissionless public-chain infrastructure — the 1,250% Basel risk weight and the TEFRA restriction operate as near-prohibitions without statutory fixes that the CLARITY Act may not resolve in its current form.

Capital flow signals: The $26.58B RWA market is almost entirely tokenised government securities and private credit — not tokenised equities. The hearing's focus on the Howey Test gap suggests the pathway to tokenised equities (the largest long-term market) is further from resolution than private credit and Treasury tokenisation. Expect capital to continue flowing toward tokenised private credit and government securities, where the legal questions are narrower.

🔄 Contrarian Take

No regulatory reform in history has catalysed that order-of-magnitude change within four years. The more realistic trajectory is $200–400B by 2030 — significant market development, but not the transformative capital markets shift the hearing implied. The bipartisan consensus on inevitability may be producing legislative ambition that outruns what courts and agencies can actually deliver in the required timeframe.

White House National AI Framework: Federal Preemption of State AI Laws, No New Agency, Seven Legislative Pillars

"Training of AI models on copyrighted material does not violate copyright laws." — White House National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence, March 20, 2026 (direct text of Framework)

On March 20, 2026, pursuant to President Trump's December 11, 2025 Executive Order on AI, the White House published its National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence — a 7-pillar legislative blueprint recommending that Congress adopt a unified federal AI regime centred on preemption of state laws and a "light-touch" regulatory posture. The Framework is advisory, not binding — it does not create legal obligations and does not direct agencies to take regulatory action — but it defines the legislative agenda that Republican congressional leadership will advance in 2026.

The seven pillars are: (1) Child Safety — safeguards against exploitation, age-assurance requirements, parental privacy controls; (2) Communities — AI infrastructure development, federal permitting streamlining, data centre cost controls; (3) Creators — copyright training data questions deferred to courts, voluntary licensing frameworks, safeguards on digital replicas; (4) Censorship — federal prohibition on government coercion of AI content moderation; (5) Competitiveness — regulatory sandboxes, federal AI dataset access, sector-specific regulation via existing regulators rather than a new overarching agency; (6) Workforce and Education — AI fluency integration into education and training systems; (7) State Law Preemption — federal standard preempting state AI laws that create "fragmented patchwork" burdens, while preserving state authority for child protection, consumer protection, and fraud prevention laws of general applicability.

The Framework's most consequential provision for commercial AI development is Pillar 7 combined with Pillar 3. The preemption language targets state laws regulating AI model development and imposing liability on AI developers for third-party misuse — California's AI Transparency Act (SB-53), Colorado's SB 205, and Texas's HB 1709 are the primary state laws in the crosshairs. The copyright declaration — that training on copyrighted material does not violate copyright law — pre-empts the judicial determination currently contested in New York Times Co. v. Microsoft Corp. (SDNY), Getty Images v. Stability AI (D. Del.), and multiple consolidated Ninth Circuit proceedings.

📎 Historical Precedent: The Communications Decency Act, Section 230 (1996) — federal preemption of state liability for internet content platforms. The analogy is apt: Congress used federal preemption to prevent a 50-state patchwork of liability rules from fragmenting the early commercial internet. The White House AI Framework proposes the same structural move for AI model development liability. The risk is the same: preemption eliminates state innovation in governance alongside state inefficiency.
⚖️ Practitioner Analysis

Regulatory trajectory: The Framework is a legislative template, not law — but it signals which provisions will survive a Republican-controlled Congress. Expect a federal AI bill to advance in the second half of 2026, with state preemption and the "no new agency" provision as the non-negotiable core. The EU AI Act high-risk rules (effective August 2026) will create transatlantic divergence on the same timeline as US federal legislation is being drafted — companies with EU exposure face dual compliance obligations that the Framework explicitly does not resolve.

Who wins: Large AI developers (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta) — preemption eliminates California's SB-53 compliance costs and the 50-state liability patchwork. Copyright holders lose the political leverage they had at the state level; their primary recourse is now federal litigation, where the Framework's copyright declaration signals the administration's brief. Who loses: California (primary enforcement mechanism undermined), state AG offices with active AI investigations, plaintiffs' attorneys in AI copyright litigation (administration position strengthens fair use defence).

Cross-jurisdictional chess: The EU is moving toward binding AI liability rules (AI Liability Directive, expected 2026–2027); the US is moving toward federal preemption of state liability rules with no new federal liability standard. The result is a regulatory asymmetry where US-headquartered AI companies face binding EU liability obligations and no US federal liability at all — making EU market access the primary governance constraint on frontier AI development.

🔄 Contrarian Take

The Framework's copyright declaration is not law — it is an administration position in a pending litigation landscape. Federal courts hearing NYT v. Microsoft and Getty v. Stability AI are not bound by White House policy papers. The declaration may actually harm AI developers by signalling that Congress supports fair use, potentially stiffening judicial resistance to a legislative override of existing copyright doctrine. Courts reading congressional intent in light of the Framework may conclude that fair use is already the law — and decide the cases on those merits, producing outcomes neither side predicted.

Expect CFTC guidance on AI trading systems before year-end.

OCC "Franken-Charter" Race: 11 Firms in 83 Days, April 1 Amendment Cements Non-Fiduciary Custody, ABA Mounts Opposition

"This structure is a 'Franken-charter' assembled from incompatible regulatory components." — American Bankers Association, characterising the OCC national trust bank charter for crypto firms (paraphrased from ABA public statement, Q1 2026)

Between December 12, 2025 and March 4, 2026 — 83 days — eleven companies filed for or received conditional approval for OCC national trust bank charters, representing the fastest wave of federal banking licence applications in the OCC's modern history. The first wave (December 12): Ripple (Ripple National Trust Bank), Circle (First National Digital Currency Bank), BitGo, Fidelity Digital Assets, and Paxos — five simultaneous conditional approvals. The second wave (February 2026): Bridge (Stripe's stablecoin subsidiary), Protego, and Crypto.com. Applications currently pending: Morgan Stanley (Morgan Stanley Digital Trust National Association), Payoneer, Zerohash, Coinbase, and World Liberty Financial.

The commercial logic is consolidation: a national trust charter provides federal legitimacy and single-regulator OCC oversight across all 50 states, eliminating the need for individual state money transmitter licences, trust licences, and the sponsor-bank model that dominated crypto infrastructure between 2020 and 2024. What the charter does not permit: deposit-taking or lending. National trust banks hold assets, manage trust relationships, and process payments — the custody and transfer functions central to stablecoin issuance and digital asset management.

On April 1, 2026 — five days from today — an OCC rule amendment takes effect clarifying that national trust banks can conduct non-fiduciary custody operations. The amendment resolves a longstanding ambiguity in the national trust charter framework that exposed charter holders to potential regulatory challenge. Legal analysis from Steptoe and others confirms the amendment makes non-fiduciary digital asset custody "resistant to future challenge" under the National Bank Act.

The ABA's "Franken-charter" characterisation is more than rhetoric: the ABA and the Conference of State Banking Supervisors have both filed formal objections arguing the OCC is creating a regulatory arbitrage vehicle — federal legitimacy without federal deposit insurance requirements, creating competitive asymmetry against FDIC-supervised banks. Watch for a legal challenge filing after April 1.

📎 Historical Precedent: OCC's Fintech Charter (2018) — the OCC attempted to create a "special purpose national bank charter" for fintech firms. The CSBS and NYDFS filed suit; federal courts in both the SDNY (2019) and D.C. Circuit (2021) rejected the charter as exceeding OCC authority when the charter holder didn't accept deposits. The national trust bank charter uses a different statutory basis — 12 U.S.C. § 92a (trust company authority) rather than the general national bank charter — but faces analogous structural legal risks.
⚖️ Practitioner Analysis

Regulatory trajectory: The April 1 non-fiduciary custody amendment cements the current wave of approvals. But the ABA legal challenge, if filed, will target the OCC's authority to approve trust banks that primarily conduct digital asset custody rather than traditional trust administration. The Fintech Charter litigation precedent (CSBS v. OCC) is the ABA's template — expect a motion for preliminary injunction targeting new approvals while litigation is pending.

Who wins: Charter holders (Ripple, Circle, BitGo, Paxos, Fidelity Digital Assets) — they gain federal pre-emption of state licensing requirements, OCC as single supervisor, and a regulatory moat against competitors without charters. Stablecoin issuers with charters can position themselves as "bank-equivalent" custodians for institutional clients. Who loses: State regulators (CSBS), traditional trust banks competing for digital asset custody mandates, and any crypto firm in the pending application queue if the ABA challenge triggers a charter review freeze.

Enforcement risk: Firms relying on the trust bank charter for state licensing pre-emption are exposed if ABA litigation succeeds. A court injunction against new charter approvals does not void existing conditional approvals, but a ruling that the OCC exceeded its authority could unwind the entire framework — the same outcome as the 2021 Fintech Charter ruling.

🏗️ Industry Implications

Circle and Ripple: Federal trust bank status directly supports their stablecoin issuance programmes under the GENIUS Act. OCC-supervised stablecoin issuers receive the most favourable regulatory treatment under the GENIUS Act framework — better reserve requirements flexibility and clearer redemption mechanics than state-chartered issuers.

Morgan Stanley: Its pending application (Morgan Stanley Digital Trust National Association) signals that traditional investment banks view digital asset custody as a core next-decade revenue line — not a bolt-on. A Morgan Stanley OCC charter would represent the largest traditional financial institution entering digital asset custody with a dedicated federal licence.

Coinbase: With a pending application, Coinbase's charter timing interacts directly with the CLARITY Act yield ban dynamics — a trust bank charter holder may face different yield prohibition treatment than a non-bank DASP. The charter application creates strategic optionality in the CLARITY Act negotiations.

MiCA Enforcement Bites: 14 Exchanges Licensed, Trading Volume Down 15%, DeFi Fills the Gap

"Exchanges that failed to secure CASP authorisation have been forced to stop serving EU customers. Stablecoins that do not meet MiCA's reserve and disclosure requirements have been delisted from compliant platforms." — ESMA enforcement summary, paraphrased from published guidance on CASP authorisation requirements, March 2026

The CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) authorisation requirement under MiCA became fully enforceable in January 2026. As of mid-March 2026, the practical effects are clear: fourteen cryptocurrency exchanges have obtained full CASP authorization across the EU. Exchanges operating without authorisation have been required to exit the EU market or restrict EU-resident access. Stablecoins failing MiCA's reserve and disclosure requirements — including several algorithmic stablecoins and certain offshore-issued USD-pegged tokens — have been delisted from compliant platforms.

The market impact data is material: spot trading volume from EU-resident users declined approximately 15% in the first two months of 2026 compared to the same period in 2025. However, volume on EU-accessible decentralised exchanges has grown, partially offsetting the regulated market decline. Licensed exchanges report higher average trade sizes — institutional and professional traders staying, while casual retail activity has migrated to either DeFi alternatives or non-EU platforms. Binance (French entity) and OKX (Maltese entity) are among the 14 licensed CASPs.

ESMA has issued guidance clarifying several ambiguous MiCA provisions: how decentralised protocols are treated (primary conclusion: truly decentralised protocols without an identifiable issuer are outside MiCA scope, but any centralised entry point creates a regulated activity), and what constitutes a "significant" stablecoin requiring enhanced oversight (threshold criteria based on user count, transaction volume, and reserve size).

📎 Historical Precedent: PSD2 (EU Payment Services Directive 2, 2018) — compliance timelines resulted in a similar first-year consolidation pattern, with 30%+ of non-compliant payment service providers exiting the EU market in year one. Volume recovered within 18 months as compliant infrastructure scaled. MiCA's 15% volume decline is within the PSD2 historical range.
⚖️ Practitioner Analysis

Regulatory trajectory: The 14-exchange licensed cohort is a fraction of the pre-MiCA European exchange ecosystem. The consolidation dynamic will continue through 2026 as smaller exchanges either obtain licences or exit. ESMA's "truly decentralised" carve-out is the most legally significant guidance to date — it creates a pathway for DeFi protocols to operate in the EU without CASP authorisation, but the "identifiable centralised entry point" standard is undefined and will be litigated.

Capital flow signals: The 15% decline in EU spot volume and DeFi growth signal that MiCA has successfully pushed retail volume toward decentralised venues — the opposite of the compliance-driven market concentration the regulation intended to achieve. Expect ESMA to propose DeFi-specific guidance or rulemaking in Q3 2026.

Regulatory arbitrage: Dubai (VARA), Singapore (MAS), and Switzerland (FINMA) are all actively marketing their regulatory frameworks as MiCA alternatives for EU-market access. Exchanges that cannot obtain or afford CASP authorisation are licensing in these jurisdictions and serving EU residents through "reverse solicitation" — a MiCA exception that ESMA has flagged as a primary enforcement concern for 2026.

🏗️ Industry Implications

Binance and OKX: Both obtained CASP licences through EU subsidiaries, securing access to the EU market but accepting ongoing ESMA oversight of their EU operations. The licence creates a compliance overhead but is worth the EU market access — approximately €28B in annual exchange revenue at stake at current rates.

Tether (USDT): USDT does not currently meet MiCA's reserve and disclosure requirements for e-money tokens. Its delisting from MiCA-compliant EU platforms is the largest supply-side shift in the stablecoin market — USDC and MiCA-compliant euro stablecoins are the primary beneficiaries of the 2026 EU market restructuring.

DeFi protocols: ESMA's "truly decentralised" carve-out provides operating room for Uniswap, Curve, and Aave in the EU — but any front-end operator or governance token holder who can be characterised as an "identifiable centralised entry point" faces CASP authorisation requirements. Expect targeted enforcement actions against DAO governance structures in H2 2026.

CFTC No-Action Relief for Self-Custody Wallets: The CFTC's Staff Letter 26-05 provides no-action relief allowing non-security digital assets held in self-custody to be used as margin collateral in derivatives accounts. Follow-up FAQs issued March 20 clarify that FCMs may apply the value of customer non-security crypto assets — after applicable haircuts — to secure debit or deficit account balances. This is the most significant liberalisation of derivatives margin rules for crypto collateral to date. Applicable to futures, foreign futures, and cleared swaps. No comment period; effective immediately.
AUSTRAC Registration Deadline — March 31 (4 Days): Australian digital asset businesses are required to register with AUSTRAC and establish formal anti-money laundering (AML) programmes by March 31, 2026. From that date, unregistered businesses operating as digital currency exchanges (DCEs) face civil penalties up to AUD 11.1M per contravention. ASIC's sector-wide no-action position on AFS licensing runs until June 30, 2026 — but the AUSTRAC registration deadline is separate and immediately enforceable. Australian-based exchanges should confirm registration is complete before end of business March 31.

Weekly Rollup — Friday, 27 March 2026

Week of 23–27 March 2026

The week delivered the sharpest commercial test of crypto regulatory progress in 2026. The SEC/CFTC joint taxonomy (March 17), which classified Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana, XRP, and 12 other major cryptocurrencies as digital commodities, entered the week as the dominant story — the formal end of regulation-by-enforcement, a milestone the industry had sought for five years. By Thursday, that milestone had been partially overshadowed by the CLARITY Act's stablecoin yield ban, which landed in leaked draft text as a more commercially destructive provision than the industry had been told to expect. Circle's single-session loss of $5.6 billion and Coinbase's public break with the bill is the clearest sign yet that regulatory finality and commercial finality are different things — and that the banking lobby, not the crypto industry, may be writing the stablecoin yield rules.

The week's second defining event was the House Tokenization Hearing (March 25), which confirmed bipartisan legislative intent around tokenised real-world assets while producing a precise inventory of the six statutory and technical barriers preventing that intent from becoming market reality. The CLARITY Act is now carrying two distinct legislative agendas simultaneously — crypto market structure and tokenisation reform — plus the stablecoin yield controversy. That is a large payload for a single bill, and the April markup will determine whether the weight collapses the vehicle or propels it.

Single most important development: The CLARITY Act stablecoin yield ban. Not because the bill will necessarily pass with this language — it may not — but because it defined the commercial fault line that will determine whether USDC and exchange-distributed stablecoins remain competitive retail products or cede ground to bank-issued stablecoins and Tether. The rulemaking window (12 months, three agencies) is the real battleground. The outcome will not be determined at markup — it will be determined in comment letters, lobbying meetings, and agency rulemaking proceedings over the next 18 months.
