Rabbit Hole

The Loan That Creates
Its Own Money

Economics 1 April 2026 · Sydney, Australia
TL;DR

Almost everything you were taught about how banks work is wrong. Banks do not collect deposits and lend them out — they create new money every time they make a loan. Your mortgage doesn't come from someone else's savings. The bank types a number into your account and that number is new money, conjured into existence at the moment of signing. The Bank of England confirmed this in an official 2014 paper that quietly rewrote mainstream economics. The money supply of an entire economy expands and contracts with the lending decisions of private banks. Most economists spent a century teaching the wrong model. Some still do.

Section 01

The Story

Start with the goldsmith. It is 17th-century London. You have gold. The goldsmith has a vault. You deposit your gold, and the goldsmith gives you a paper receipt — a promise to return the gold on demand. You can use that receipt to pay for things; the merchant who accepts it knows it's good for gold at the vault. The receipt is money.

Now the goldsmith notices something. Depositors don't all come for their gold at the same time. On any given day, only a fraction of receipts get redeemed. So he starts issuing more receipts than he has gold. He lends these extra receipts out, at interest. As long as he keeps enough gold on hand to satisfy normal daily demands, nobody is the wiser. He has just invented fractional reserve banking — and, more importantly, he has invented the creation of money from nothing.

This is not a story about fraud, though it felt like one to critics at the time. It is the origin story of the entire modern financial system. The goldsmiths' innovation was formalised when the Bank of England was chartered in 1694 — itself created specifically to lend money to the Crown that didn't technically exist yet — and it has been the operating principle of banking ever since.

But here is what most people — including most economics students — still get wrong: the popular image of a bank is a savings warehouse. Deposits come in, loans go out. The bank is a middleman moving money from savers to borrowers. This model is intuitive, tidy, and almost completely false.

🔍 In Plain English — The Textbook Model vs. Reality

The textbook says: banks collect deposits, keep a fraction in reserve, and lend the rest. If the reserve requirement is 10%, a $1,000 deposit allows $900 in loans, which become new deposits, which allow $810 in loans, and so on — until the original $1,000 has "multiplied" to $10,000 in money supply. This is the money multiplier model. It is the version in nearly every introductory economics textbook. It is not how modern banking actually works.

In March 2014, the Bank of England published a paper in its Quarterly Bulletin that stated the case with unusual bluntness. Written by economists Michael McLeay, Amar Radia, and Ryland Thomas, it opened with a declaration that felt almost like a confession: "The reality of how money is created today differs from the description found in some economics textbooks."

The paper explained that the vast majority of money in the modern economy is created by commercial banks — not by central banks printing notes, and not by the mechanical multiplication of deposits. When a commercial bank makes a loan, it simply credits the borrower's account. It does not transfer money from anywhere. It creates a new deposit — new money — at the moment of the loan. The loan and the deposit are born simultaneously, as two sides of the same accounting entry.

This was not a new theory. Post-Keynesian economists had argued for it for decades. But this was the Bank of England — the oldest central bank in the world, the institution that governs UK monetary policy — putting it in writing, in plain language, in an official publication. The economics blogosphere treated it as a bombshell. The mainstream largely absorbed it quietly and moved on.

Section 02

The Mechanism

Walk through a mortgage to see how this actually works. You want to buy a house for $800,000. You go to a bank. The bank approves your loan. What happens next is not that the bank checks its vault, finds $800,000 of depositors' savings, and transfers them to the seller. What happens is this: the bank creates a new loan on its asset ledger (you owe us $800,000) and simultaneously creates a new deposit in the seller's account (here is $800,000). Both entries appear from nothing. New money has entered the economy.

The double-entry bookkeeping looks like this: on the bank's balance sheet, the loan is an asset (it generates future income) and the new deposit is a liability (the bank owes it to the depositor). They cancel each other out, so the bank's equity hasn't changed. But money supply has grown by $800,000.

This is why the money multiplier model is a myth. In that model, banks need deposits first — you deposit money, and the bank lends from that pool. In reality, banks lend first and deposits appear as a consequence. As the Bank of England paper puts it: "loans create deposits."

What actually limits how much a bank can lend? Several things, but not reserve requirements in the way textbooks claim. In practice, lending is constrained by:

1. Credit risk. Banks only lend to borrowers they believe can repay. If the economy looks shaky, they lend less — not because they run out of money, but because they are unwilling to take on bad assets.

2. Capital requirements. Regulators require banks to hold equity capital proportional to their lending. More loans require more capital. This is the real binding constraint in modern banking, not reserve ratios.

3. Monetary policy. The central bank sets interest rates, which determines the cost of borrowing. Higher rates make loans more expensive and dampen demand. Lower rates encourage borrowing and expand money supply.

4. Demand for loans. Banks can only create money if someone wants to borrow. When businesses are pessimistic, they don't invest; when households are scared, they don't borrow. A bank can't force money creation if there are no creditworthy borrowers.

In 2014 — the same year as the Bank of England paper — economist Richard Werner published the first formal empirical test of the competing theories of banking, borrowing money from a small cooperative bank in Germany while monitoring its internal accounts. He confirmed directly: the bank created a new deposit ex nihilo when it booked the loan. No internal transfer occurred. No pre-existing funds were moved. The credit creation theory of banking won the empirical test conclusively.

🔍 In Plain English — Where Money Goes When a Loan Is Repaid

When you repay a loan, the opposite happens: the deposit shrinks and the loan (asset) disappears. Money is destroyed. This is why recessions are so brutal — when loans go bad and lending contracts, the money supply itself contracts. Deflation can become self-reinforcing: less money means less spending, less revenue, more defaults, more contraction. The Great Depression was, in large part, a catastrophic collapse of private money creation.

Section 03
The Connections

Once you understand that private banks create money, a lot of things snap into focus. The 2008 financial crisis was not just a crash in house prices — it was a sudden, catastrophic contraction in the money supply as mortgage-backed lending collapsed. The central banks' frantic asset-purchasing programs (QE) were an attempt to fill the hole left when private money creation stopped. Bitcoin's entire value proposition becomes legible: a fixed-supply asset that cannot be created by a private institution typing numbers into a ledger. Modern Monetary Theory's controversial claim that governments don't "run out" of money makes more sense when you see that money is already conjured into existence by private banks every day. The question of who controls money creation — states, banks, algorithms, or something else — is the most consequential political question of the next century. Most people don't know they should be asking it.

Section 04

Further Down the Hole

History The Goldsmiths Who Invented Modern Money

In 17th-century London, goldsmiths like Edward Backwell and Francis Child evolved from jewellers into the first private bankers — issuing paper receipts that circulated as currency and discovering that they could issue more receipts than gold. The Bank of England's 1694 charter was explicitly designed to formalise and regulate what they had already been doing. The modern $100 trillion global money system descends directly from their vaults.

Anthropology David Graeber's Debt: The First 5,000 Years

Graeber, an anthropologist and anarchist, argued that debt — not barter, not commodity exchange — is the original economic relationship. He traced credit systems to ancient Mesopotamia, predating coins by millennia. His provocative conclusion: money has never been a neutral medium of exchange. It has always been a social relationship encoded in numbers. The Bank of England paper, published the year before Graeber died, vindicated much of his argument through orthodox channels.

Experiment Richard Werner's Bank Test

In 2014, Werner borrowed a small sum from a German cooperative bank (Raiffeisenbank Wildenberg) while being allowed to observe its internal bookkeeping. When the loan was approved, he watched the bank's accounts. No funds were moved from reserves. No existing deposit was transferred. A new number appeared in his account — new money, created at the moment of the loan. It was the first empirical confirmation in banking history of what economists had debated theoretically for over a century.

Policy Central Bank Digital Currencies: The End of Commercial Money?

If central banks issue digital currencies (CBDCs) directly to citizens — bypassing commercial banks — they would, for the first time since the goldsmith era, reclaim the power to create money from private institutions. Most major central banks are actively developing CBDCs. China's digital yuan is already in broad circulation. The implications are staggering: perfect monetary control, instant payment rails, and the potential to make commercial bank money creation obsolete. Or a surveillance tool of unprecedented scope. Possibly both.

Crypto Bitcoin as a Repudiation of Endogenous Money

Satoshi Nakamoto's 2008 whitepaper arrived during the height of the financial crisis — when private money creation had just imploded spectacularly. Bitcoin's fixed supply of 21 million coins is the antithesis of a system where banks create money at will. No bank, government, or algorithm can increase the supply. It is the goldsmith's receipt if the goldsmith were mathematically incapable of issuing more receipts than gold. Whether that rigidity is a feature or a bug in an economy that needs flexible money supply is the central debate in crypto economics.
