SEC-CFTC Joint Crypto Asset Taxonomy: Five Categories, Eighteen Digital Commodities, Securities Law Lifted from Bitcoin, ETH, Solana and XRP

"Today, I am pleased to announce that the SEC's persistent failure to provide clarity on this question is over." — SEC Chair Paul Atkins, The Digital Chamber's Blockchain Summit, March 17, 2026

On March 17, 2026, the Securities and Exchange Commission issued Release No. 33-11412 — a 68-page interpretive guidance document that, for the first time in the agency's history, definitively answers which crypto assets are securities and which are not. The Commodity Futures Trading Commission joined the release simultaneously, providing guidance that it will administer the Commodity Exchange Act consistent with the SEC's interpretation.

The framework establishes five categories: digital commodities (not securities — CFTC jurisdiction), digital collectibles (NFTs — narrow securities analysis), digital utilities (platform-native tokens with functional use), stablecoins (distinct regime), and digital securities (full Howey securities law application). Eighteen major assets are explicitly identified as digital commodities, including Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana, XRP, Dogecoin, Cardano, Avalanche, Chainlink, Polkadot, Hedera, Litecoin, Bitcoin Cash, Shiba Inu, Stellar, Tezos, and Aptos.

The document addresses four long-contested edge cases: how a non-security crypto asset may become subject to an investment contract analysis (issuer representations or promises of profits from essential managerial efforts), how it may later cease to be subject to those laws, and how federal securities laws apply to airdrops, protocol mining, protocol staking, and token wrapping. The guidance explicitly supersedes all prior SEC staff statements on crypto assets, including the 2019 FinHub framework, without retroactive effect on existing enforcement actions or litigation.

The guidance was paired with an SEC-CFTC Memorandum of Understanding and the formation of "Project Crypto," a joint regulatory harmonisation initiative. Industry reaction was overwhelmingly positive: Coinbase CLO Paul Grewal said on X that "2023 me couldn't have imagined that 2026 me would see such a thing." The XRP designation as digital commodity formally ends seven years of Ripple's existential litigation with the SEC, opening the door to US ETP applications and further exchange listings.

📎 Historical Precedent: The 1992 CFTC-SEC jurisdictional split over stock index futures (Shad-Johnson Accord) took a decade to negotiate and was ultimately codified by Congress in the Commodity Futures Modernization Act of 2000 — the closest analogue to a regulator-driven jurisdictional clarity milestone of this magnitude.
⚖️ PRACTITIONER ANALYSIS

The taxonomy operates as interpretive guidance, not a rule — it is not subject to notice-and-comment and can be revised without APA process. However, by explicitly naming 18 assets as digital commodities, the SEC has created reliance interests that will be extraordinarily politically costly to walk back. The five-category framework solves the Howey application problem for established assets but creates new uncertainty for mid-cap tokens not explicitly named. Tokens with active "managerial representations" from issuers remain exposed to digital securities classification.

The investment contract analysis survives: issuers that continue making forward-looking profit representations about token appreciation retain full securities exposure regardless of the commodity/security base classification. This matters for DeFi protocols where founders routinely communicate publicly about protocol roadmaps and yield projections. The CFTC's simultaneous endorsement is legally significant — it forecloses forum shopping and agency-switching arguments that sophisticated defendants have deployed to generate jurisdictional uncertainty.

Winners: Coinbase (no longer a securities exchange for 18 assets), Kraken, Ripple (XRP litigation appeal becomes moot), Solana ecosystem projects. Losers: SEC enforcement pipeline (dozens of pending cases must be reviewed against the new taxonomy), and any issuer whose token sits in the contested "digital security" bucket with no pathway to reclassification.

🔄 CONTRARIAN TAKE

Interpretive guidance is not a rule, and the next administration can repudiate it on day one without APA process. The 18-asset "safe list" may also create perverse incentives — issuers of unlisted tokens will now lobby for inclusion, and the line between a "digital utility" and a "digital security" remains vague enough that the next enforcement-minded SEC chair has ample room to reclassify. The guidance's explicit carve-out for ongoing litigation means XRP holders and other defendants get no benefit from the new taxonomy in their existing cases.

SEC Chair Atkins Proposes "Regulation Crypto Assets" — Three Fundraising Pathways Including a Token Safe Harbor for Mature Networks

"It is past time for us to stop diagnosing the problem and start delivering the solution. Such a safe harbor would provide crypto innovators bespoke pathways to raise capital in the US, while providing appropriate investor protections." — SEC Chair Paul Atkins, Blockchain Summit remarks, March 17, 2026

On March 17, simultaneously with the release of Release No. 33-11412, SEC Chair Atkins outlined a potential framework he called "Regulation Crypto Assets" — a proposed suite of exemptions and safe harbors designed to give crypto projects structured pathways to raise capital in the United States without triggering full registration requirements under the Securities Act of 1933.

The framework has three components: a startup exemption permitting early-stage crypto projects to offer tokens under modified disclosure standards analogous to Regulation Crowdfunding; a fundraising exemption allowing token sales to accredited and institutional investors without registration, akin to Regulation D but with crypto-specific investor protection overlays; and an investment contract safe harbor for tokens issued on mature, sufficiently decentralised networks — a long-demanded mechanism first proposed by then-Commissioner Hester Peirce in 2019 as "Token Safe Harbor 2.0".

The safe harbor concept addresses the core regulatory gap: issuers of tokens that are initially sold as investment contracts can exit securities treatment once their network reaches functional decentralisation thresholds, defined by reference to active users, transaction volume, and issuer control diminution. Atkins framed the framework as responsive to years of industry feedback that the US capital markets were losing token issuance deals to Switzerland, Singapore, and the Cayman Islands.

📎 Historical Precedent: Commissioner Peirce's original "Token Safe Harbor 2.0" proposal (April 2021) was rejected under the Gensler SEC; Atkins is reviving and expanding the concept with executive backing for the first time.
⚖️ PRACTITIONER ANALYSIS

The safe harbor proposal is aspirational — it requires either formal rulemaking (subject to APA notice-and-comment) or an interpretive release, neither of which has yet been filed. Atkins signalled he intends to pursue formal rulemaking to give the framework legal durability that interpretive guidance cannot provide. The decentralisation threshold metrics remain the hardest drafting problem: defining "functional decentralisation" quantitatively invites gaming and will generate significant comment period controversy from issuers seeking to minimise their path to safe harbor qualification.

For existing securities-adjacent tokens, the investment contract safe harbor is the most commercially important pathway. Ethereum's trajectory — sold as a security-adjacent instrument in 2014, ultimately treated as a commodity — provides the precedent but not a rule-based mechanism. Atkins is trying to codify that evolution prospectively. Winners: token issuers with large US retail communities who have been legally blocked from direct-to-US distribution. Losers: offshore legal jurisdictions that have captured US-origin crypto capital formation for the past six years.

🔄 CONTRARIAN TAKE

Safe harbor proposals from SEC chairs have a graveyard of predecessors (see: Regulation A+, Regulation S "lite", Peirce's prior token proposals). The startup and fundraising exemptions are structurally similar to Reg CF and Reg D, which already apply to crypto — Atkins may be creating compliance complexity rather than reducing it. And a safe harbor that requires rulemaking will take 18–24 months to finalise, leaving the current uncertainty intact for the duration of the Trump administration's crypto-friendly window.

🏗️ INDUSTRY IMPLICATIONS

Token issuers: US capital formation now structurally feasible for the first time since 2017. Expect a wave of SAFT 3.0 instruments designed to qualify under the fundraising exemption pathway while retaining safe harbor optionality. a16z crypto, Paradigm, and Multicoin Capital are immediately better positioned to lead US-listed token rounds.

Securities law practice: Cooley, Goodwin, and Latham will build dedicated "Reg Crypto" practices; expect CLE programmes and formal opinion infrastructure to emerge within 60 days. The three-pathway framework will require bespoke documentation for each path.

Offshore jurisdictions: The Cayman Islands, British Virgin Islands, and Switzerland (FINMA) will face token deal flow competition from a newly hospitable US market — particularly for institutional rounds where US investors have been excluded to avoid securities law triggers.

📊 PORTFOLIO IMPACT

Bullish: US-based venture capital with crypto exposure (Coinbase Ventures, a16z crypto); legal technology platforms serving securities compliance; exchange tokens for compliant US platforms (COIN).
Bearish: Offshore legal structuring service providers; jurisdictions (Cayman, BVI) that profited from US capital formation flight.
Neutral: Near-term — rules not yet filed. Medium-term bullish for early-stage token ecosystems.

CLARITY Act "Inches Toward" Senate Banking Markup — Stablecoin Yield Deal in Place, April Target Floated, Four Hard Issues Unresolved

"Sen. Tillis and I do have an agreement in principle. We've come a long way. And I think what it will do is to allow us to protect innovation, but also gives us the opportunity to prevent widespread deposit flight." — Sen. Angela Alsobrooks (D-MD), Politico, March 20, 2026

The Digital Asset Market Clarity Act (H.R. 3633), which passed the House 294-134 in July 2025 and was stalled in the Senate Banking Committee since January after a postponed markup, has moved incrementally closer to a Senate committee vote following a breakthrough on stablecoin yield on March 20. Senators Thom Tillis (R-NC) and Angela Alsobrooks (D-MD) publicly confirmed an agreement in principle on the provision that caused the January markup cancellation.

The Tillis-Alsobrooks compromise prohibits passive holding rewards — yield paid simply for holding a stablecoin balance without any associated activity — but permits activity-based rewards tied to payments, transfers, and platform usage. Senator Lummis's press team described the negotiations as "99% resolved" in late March. White House Crypto Council Executive Director Patrick Witt called it a "major milestone." However, industry analysts have identified four remaining hard issues: DeFi protocol treatment, ethics rules for crypto-owning Members of Congress, CFTC appointment and structure provisions, and the recent emergence of a community bank deregulation attachment that several Democratic senators have threatened to use as a dealbreaker.

The Senate Banking Committee has not posted a markup date. Sen. Lummis and others have flagged April as the target window but have not committed. The realistic floor vote window is May through June 2026, before midterm election dynamics absorb Senate floor time. The bill requires 60 votes to overcome a filibuster, meaning at least seven Democrats must cross over.

📎 Historical Precedent: The Bank Secrecy Act's Fincen amendments took three separate reconciliation attempts before clearing a conference committee in 2001 — the closest analogue to a multi-issue crypto bill requiring bipartisan supermajority coalition-building.
⚖️ PRACTITIONER ANALYSIS

The stablecoin yield deal solves the most commercially significant provision for Circle (USDC), Paxos (USDP), and Tether — each of which would have faced different market dynamics under a passive yield prohibition vs. an activity-based reward model. The activity-based reward model preserves DeFi yield mechanics (lending, liquidity provision) while closing the "stablecoin-as-savings-account" bank substitution risk that drove Democratic opposition. This is an elegant compromise that may hold.

The DeFi treatment issue is harder. The House bill's DeFi provisions drew opposition from both the DeFi Education Fund (too restrictive) and traditional bank regulators (too permissive). Any Senate compromise must thread a needle between opposing interests that are genuinely incompatible. The ethics attachment — requiring Members who own crypto to recuse from crypto legislation votes — is a political poison pill that can be weaponised by either side at any time. Expect this to re-emerge as a procedural tactic during floor debate regardless of committee resolution.

🔄 CONTRARIAN TAKE

The CLARITY Act has been "close" for 18 months. The community bank deregulation attachment — reported by CoinInsider on March 26 — suggests that Republican leadership may be using the CLARITY Act vehicle to advance unrelated banking industry priorities, which could fracture the bipartisan coalition. If the bill gains non-crypto attachments, Democratic co-sponsors lose their ability to defend the package to their constituencies, and the entire bill could collapse even after the yield deal survives.

🏗️ INDUSTRY IMPLICATIONS

Circle (USDC): Activity-based yield model is workable for USDC's DeFi integrations; Circle's institutional prime model is unaffected by the passive holding prohibition. Positive for Circle's anticipated IPO trajectory.

Coinbase: CLARITY Act codification is less urgent after SEC-CFTC taxonomy guidance but remains commercially significant for legal certainty on exchange operations. A markup failure would accelerate Coinbase's EU/APAC expansion as regulatory arbitrage.

DeFi protocols: The stalled DeFi provisions create an asymmetric risk — if the bill passes with restrictive DeFi language, major protocols (Uniswap, Aave, dYdX) will restructure offshore. If it passes with permissive language, DeFi re-onshores to US-accessible liquidity.

📊 PORTFOLIO IMPACT

Bullish on markup announcement: COIN, broad crypto market, DeFi tokens on markup date. Activity-based stablecoin yield model benefits USDC and USDP more than USDT.
Bearish on collapse: Any collapse will generate a short-term sell signal for crypto-adjacent equities.
Neutral: GENIUS Act (already law) — no incremental impact from CLARITY Act progress on stablecoin payment infrastructure.

📋 WHAT TO DO

Australia VASP Regime Activates Monday — CDD Obligations Go Live March 31, Dual-Track ASIC/AUSTRAC Enforcement Framework Begins, Travel Rule Follows July 1

"We are very aware that we're asking a lot of industry right now. But the Australian community deserves the protections that AML/CTF compliance provides, and virtual asset service providers are now part of that system." [paraphrased] — AUSTRAC spokesperson, reported in AML/CTF transitional rules update, March 2026

Effective March 31, 2026 — Monday — all existing digital currency exchange providers in Australia are reclassified as Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs) under the Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorism Financing Act 2006 (as amended by the AML/CTF Act 2024 tranche reforms). From this date, VASPs must implement ongoing Customer Due Diligence (CDD) obligations, maintain transaction monitoring programmes, and report suspicious matters to AUSTRAC under the updated definitions.

March 31 also marks the opening of the VASP enrollment period: newly regulated virtual asset service providers must enroll with AUSTRAC by July 29, 2026. Concurrently, ASIC's expanded supervisory mandate over crypto platforms under the proposed Corporations Amendment (Digital Assets Framework) Bill 2025 — endorsed by the Senate Committee on March 16 — is advancing toward a legislative vote, creating a dual-track regulatory exposure: AUSTRAC for AML/CTF enforcement, ASIC for conduct and consumer protection.

The Travel Rule itself — requiring VASPs to transmit originator and beneficiary information with virtual asset transfers — was postponed from March 31 to July 1, 2026, giving industry an additional 90 days to implement technical infrastructure. However, the CDD upgrade and transaction monitoring obligations that activate Monday are substantial operational requirements that many smaller DCE operators may not be prepared to meet.

ASIC's "economic substance" test — flagged by Rhys Bollen, ASIC's head of fintech, at the Melbourne Money and Finance Conference — means that regulatory classification of crypto activities in Australia will be function-based, not form-based. A DeFi protocol facilitating Australian retail users faces the same ASIC scrutiny as a centralised exchange, regardless of where it is technically incorporated.

📎 Historical Precedent: Australia's original AUSTRAC DCE registration regime (2018) saw 28% of registered providers exit the market within 12 months of enhanced compliance requirements — a precedent suggesting a similar consolidation wave among smaller VASPs from April 2026.
⚖️ PRACTITIONER ANALYSIS

The March 31 CDD activation creates immediate enforcement exposure for VASPs that have not updated their AML/CTF programmes. AUSTRAC's enforcement track record (see: Binance Australia AUD 10M penalty for wholesale investor misclassification, covered in prior Brief editions) demonstrates the agency's willingness to pursue material penalties for compliance failures by major players — and a proportionality-adjusted enforcement appetite for smaller operators. The dual-regime exposure is the key structural risk: non-compliance with CDD obligations triggers AUSTRAC civil penalties under AML/CTF Act s.175 (up to AUD 186,000 per breach) AND potential ASIC action under the Corporations Act for conduct breaches.

The July 1 Travel Rule deadline creates a second compliance sprint immediately following the March 31 CDD activation. VASPs that are scrambling on CDD now will be simultaneously building Travel Rule technical infrastructure. Operators with fewer than 20 compliance staff face genuine capacity constraints that may force consolidation or exit. The AUD 10M AFSL threshold in the Digital Assets Framework Bill means operators below that volume can defer AFSL licensing while remaining subject to AUSTRAC obligations — creating an asymmetric compliance burden for mid-sized operators.

🔄 CONTRARIAN TAKE

Australia's dual-track AUSTRAC/ASIC regime may actually be the global compliance best-practice model rather than an undue burden. Separating AML/CTF enforcement (AUSTRAC) from consumer protection and market conduct (ASIC) creates cleaner regulatory accountability with less mission-creep than single-regulator models. The 90-day Travel Rule postponement to July 1 also shows regulatory pragmatism — AUSTRAC is listening to industry readiness concerns.

🏗️ INDUSTRY IMPLICATIONS

Independent Reserve, CoinSpot, BTC Markets: Australian-native exchanges face competitive advantage over offshore competitors who must now either comply or exit the Australian market. Compliance costs are burdensome but the enforcement moat against non-compliant offshore competitors is real.

Binance Australia: Already penalised AUD 10M for wholesale misclassification; now entering Travel Rule and CDD obligations simultaneously. The risk of further enforcement action during this transition period is material.

DeFi protocols with AU user exposure: ASIC's economic substance test creates jurisdictional exposure for Uniswap, dYdX, and Aave if they have material Australian retail user bases. Geo-blocking Australian IP addresses is the standard offshore protocol response but may not satisfy ASIC's "facilitation" test.

📊 PORTFOLIO IMPACT

Bullish: Compliance technology providers (Chainalysis, Elliptic, TRM Labs — all active in AU market); established AU exchanges (Independent Reserve, BTC Markets) that are already AUSTRAC-compliant.
Bearish: Small AU DCE operators who may exit rather than invest in CDD/Travel Rule infrastructure; offshore exchanges with AU user exposure and no local compliance infrastructure.
Neutral: Major CEXes (Coinbase, Kraken) with existing global AML/CTF frameworks — incremental compliance cost only.

The CASS regime is the bedrock of how we protect investors in traditional finance, and it must function effectively for crypto." On March 6, 2026, the FCA published Consultation Paper CP26/8 (Quarterly Consultation No. 51), which includes a crypto-specific chapter proposing amendments to the Client Assets Sourcebook (CASS) to ensure FCA client protection rules work correctly for the new cryptoasset regulated activities regime. Comments are due April 13, 2026 on most provisions, with April 20 for client money segregation rules. The FCA also confirmed that the pre-application support service (PASS) for cryptoasset authorisation will open in July 2026, and the formal gateway application period runs September 30, 2026 to February 28, 2027.

CP26/8 follows the earlier CP26/4 (deadline March 12, now closed) which addressed Consumer Duty, Senior Managers and Certification Regime, and conduct-of-business standards for crypto firms. Together, the two consultations complete the FCA's internal Handbook readiness package for the October 2027 regime commencement date. The underlying statutory instrument — the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Cryptoassets) Regulations 2026 — was made by Parliament on February 4, 2026.

📎 Historical Precedent: CASS amendments for electronic money institutions in 2017 required three rounds of consultation before the FCA finalised rules that firms could operationalise — UK crypto firms should anticipate a similar iterative process.

The CASS consultation is technically important but strategically secondary — it is the plumbing of the new regime, not the architecture. UK-based exchanges and custody providers should begin PASS preparation now rather than waiting for September.

MEDIUM US District Court / DeFi Education Fund PRIMARY DeFi LITIGATION

Risley v. Universal Navigation: Federal Court Dismisses State Law Claims Against DeFi Protocol Developer, Reinforcing Developer Immunity for Neutral Infrastructure

"Developers of neutral financial infrastructure should not face liability for misconduct by third parties simply because they built the underlying protocol." [paraphrased from court holding] — US District Court, Risley v. Universal Navigation Inc., March 2, 2026

On March 2, 2026, a federal court dismissed state law claims in Risley v. Universal Navigation Inc., a class action suit brought against the developers of the Uniswap protocol. The dismissal reinforces the principle — established in the earlier Uniswap Labs District Court ruling in 2024 — that protocol developers are not liable for third-party misconduct facilitated by neutral financial infrastructure they built. The ruling dismissed claims that rested on tort theories of secondary liability: aiding and abetting fraud, negligent enablement, and unfair trade practices under state consumer protection statutes.

The decision is significant because plaintiffs had specifically pleaded state law claims to circumvent the federal securities law holdings that protected Uniswap Labs at the district court level. The court held that the same "neutral infrastructure" analysis that defeated securities claims also defeats state tort claims — the developers did not control the third-party actors who committed the alleged frauds, did not profit from those specific transactions, and did not hold themselves out as responsible for protocol usage. The ruling creates a persuasive precedent for state law claims in other jurisdictions and for similar suits against Aave, Compound, and Curve protocol developers.

📎 Historical Precedent: Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (1996) established an analogous principle for platform immunity against third-party content — courts are applying similar neutrality analysis to DeFi protocol developers.
⚖️ PRACTITIONER ANALYSIS

The ruling is developer-friendly but narrowly scoped. It addresses state tort claims against developers of non-custodial protocols — the court was careful to note that the developer had no ability to freeze, reverse, or selectively block transactions. DeFi protocols with admin keys, upgrade mechanisms, or fee switches face a meaningfully different fact pattern and should not assume Risley immunity applies to them. The ruling also does not address CFTC enforcement for commodities fraud facilitated by DeFi protocols — a separate legal theory with distinct case law.

🏗️ INDUSTRY IMPLICATIONS

Uniswap Labs: A further layer of legal protection against state-court class action liability. Aave, Compound, Curve protocol devs: Persuasive authority for their own state law defence postures. DeFi DAO governance token holders: Ruling does not protect DAOs with governance participation in protocol decisions — the neutral infrastructure shield does not extend to active governance participants.

MAS Single-Currency Stablecoin Framework on Track for Mid-2026 Enactment — StraitsX and Paxos Digital Singapore Positioned as First Authorised Issuers

"MAS seeks to anchor well-regulated stablecoin activities in Singapore and provide a clear framework for holders to have confidence that MAS-regulated stablecoins will maintain their value against the relevant currencies." [paraphrased from MAS framework release] — Monetary Authority of Singapore, SCS Framework implementation note, Q1 2026

The Monetary Authority of Singapore's Single-Currency Stablecoin (SCS) Regulatory Framework — finalised in August 2023 and structured under amendments to the Payment Services Act — is on track for full legislative enactment in mid-2026. The framework applies to SGD-pegged and G10-currency-pegged stablecoins issued in Singapore, requiring 100% reserves in the peg currency (cash, equivalents, or three-month government debt), segregated reserve accounts, monthly independent attestations, annual audits, minimum base capital, and par redemption within five business days.

StraitsX (the Singapore-native XSGD and XUSD issuer) and Paxos Digital Singapore are widely expected to become the first issuers to obtain the "MAS-regulated" stablecoin label upon enactment. The label is commercially significant: MAS-regulated stablecoins qualify for use in Singapore's Project Orchid pilots, the wholesale CBDC settlement infrastructure, and payment service integrations that require regulatory endorsement. In parallel, MAS confirmed in Q1 2026 that it will conduct a tokenised Singapore Government Securities (SGS) trial, settled using a wholesale central bank digital currency — deepening Singapore's positioning as the global RWA tokenisation hub.

📎 Historical Precedent: Hong Kong's stablecoin licensing framework (launched December 2025) required 14 months from consultation close to first authorisation — Singapore's timeline suggests first MAS-regulated stablecoins operational Q3-Q4 2026.
