TL;DR — Driving Thesis

This week's dominant signal is a convergence of volume-over-intensity confirmation across two major domains: training science and metabolic health. The ACSM's landmark 2026 guidelines — first update in 17 years, synthesising 137 reviews and 30,000 participants — settle the load debate: it's not how heavy you lift but how many weekly sets you accumulate (≥10 per muscle group) that drives hypertrophy, and nearly any modality gets you there. Simultaneously, a Nature Aging paper on phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) maps a new metabolic mechanism for inflammaging: a glycolytic metabolite that peaks in mid-life then progressively collapses, activating the cGAS-STING inflammatory pathway. The practical upshot — sustaining glycolytic activity through consistent exercise and adequate carbohydrate metabolism may be more protective than previously understood. For dc specifically: your PPL program is already evidence-aligned if weekly set counts hit the ≥10 threshold per muscle; the creatine anti-inflammatory case is weaker than sold — muscle and brain benefits hold, but don't count on it for systemic inflammation.

Section 01 Health
⚡ PEP as an Endogenous Brake on Inflammaging — Nature Aging 2026
[Mechanistic][Observational]

This is the most significant mechanistic paper of Q1 2026 in longevity science. Song et al. (Nature Aging, March 2026) demonstrate that phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) — a glycolytic intermediate sitting upstream of pyruvate — directly inhibits the cGAS-STING innate immune pathway by competitively binding to cGAS. The cGAS-STING axis is increasingly understood as the molecular trigger of inflammaging: it gets activated by cytosolic DNA (from senescent cells, mitochondrial damage) and drives the chronic, low-grade inflammation that accelerates aging and neurodegeneration.

The critical finding: PEP follows a biphasic trajectory across the lifespan — it rises in early adulthood, peaks, then progressively collapses. The collapse tracks almost perfectly with the onset of inflammaging. In mice, blocking PEP accumulation accelerated aging phenotypes; PEP administration before its natural decline promoted healthy aging. In human cohorts, high PEP levels correlated strongly with lower systemic inflammation and healthier aging traits.

The implication is not "take PEP as a supplement" — it's not bioavailable in that form. The takeaway is that anything that sustains glycolytic flux — regular moderate-intensity exercise, adequate carbohydrate availability around training — keeps PEP from collapsing prematurely. This gives a mechanistic explanation for why sedentary aging is so metabolically catastrophic. The cGAS-STING story also connects to the established benefits of NAD+ precursors and senolytics — multiple upstream interventions may be working through this same pathway.

The human correlation data is observational and needs prospective trials before interventional conclusions are drawn. But the mechanistic mouse data is compelling enough to watch closely.

Creatine's Anti-Inflammatory Case Collapses Under RCT Scrutiny
[Meta]

A new systematic review and meta-analysis published in Frontiers in Immunology (February 2026) — 8 double-blind RCTs analysing CRP, IL-6, IL-1β, TNF-α, and prostaglandin E2 — delivers a clear verdict: creatine supplementation does not significantly reduce inflammatory biomarkers in humans. Some benefit appeared under intense endurance conditions, but results were inconsistent across populations.

The muscle performance and cognitive benefits of creatine remain rock-solid (multiple independent meta-analyses, 30+ years of data). But the anti-inflammatory narrative being pushed in 2025-2026 supplement marketing is not supported by human RCT evidence. Don't modify your creatine protocol — 3–5g/day for muscle and cognition still makes sense — but don't expect it to meaningfully reduce systemic inflammation.
⚡Delta: Creatine's anti-inflammatory claim is now actively contradicted by a formal meta-analysis. Adjust expectations accordingly.
Sleep: Daily Exercise Cadence Outperforms Intensity for Sleep Quality
[Meta]

A 2026 systematic review in ScienceDirect on aerobic exercise and sleep quality, combined with UT Austin research, confirms that daily exercise frequency — not intensity — is the primary driver of improved sleep architecture. The evidence-based prescription: low-to-moderate aerobic exercise, ≥3 sessions/week (ideally daily), ≥30 minutes per session, sustained ≥10 weeks, cumulative weekly volume exceeding 90 minutes. The mechanism: exercise-induced adenosine accumulation increases sleep pressure; daily exercise prevents adenosine clearance from resetting fully, building a deeper homeostatic sleep drive.

For a swing trader managing irregular hours and market stress, sleep quality is directly tied to decision quality. Daily Zone 2 or moderate cardio — even a 30-minute walk — will do more for sleep than any supplement. If sleep quality is degraded, check training frequency before reaching for magnesium or glycine (both still valid, but secondary).
Zone 2 Intensity: Individual Variability Larger Than Expected
[Observational]

A PMC study on Zone 2 intensity markers found coefficients of variation of 6–29% across standard Zone 2 identification methods (% HRmax, blood lactate thresholds, VT1). The most reliable markers were Ventilatory Threshold 1 (VT1) and maximal fat oxidation — fixed HR percentages showed the widest individual spread. The practical read: if you're using 60-70% HRmax as your Zone 2 cutoff, you may be significantly off your actual aerobic threshold in either direction.

The gold standard remains a metabolic test (VO2 or lactate). As a practical proxy, the "conversational pace" test — you can speak in full sentences but feel mild effort — correlates better with VT1 than any fixed HR formula. Wearable-estimated Zone 2 is a starting point, not a prescription.
⚡ Highest-leverage health action: Prioritise daily moderate exercise (Zone 2 / brisk walk) to sustain glycolytic flux — this is now mechanistically linked to inflammaging prevention via the PEP/cGAS-STING pathway, not just cardiovascular benefit.
Section 02 Biohacking
Psychedelics + Neuroplasticity: The Molecule-vs-Experience Debate Sharpens
[Expert Opinion]

Tim Ferriss (5-Bullet Friday, March 20) flagged a New Yorker piece by ER physician and UC Berkeley Psychedelic Journalism Fellow Clayton Dalton, examining the "neuroplastogen" approach — stripping hallucinations from psychedelic molecules to produce scalable, insurance-compatible mental health treatments. Ferriss himself is funding research into psychedelics' anti-inflammatory effects at sub-hallucinogenic doses, which he describes as "profound, even at miniscule doses."

The debate: do the subjective experiences (set, setting, trip content) drive the therapeutic benefit for PTSD and alcohol-use disorder — or can physiological mechanisms (anti-inflammation, BDNF upregulation, neuroplasticity) work without the experience? Ferriss' view: conditions like treatment-resistant depression and chronic anxiety may not always need the trip, while trauma-specific conditions like PTSD likely do.

This is a legitimate emerging area, but the regulatory and access timeline in Australia remains long. What's practically relevant now: breathwork (especially cyclic sighing and box breathing) activates overlapping neuroplasticity mechanisms via the same PFC modulation pathways. Low-cost, legal, evidence-supported. The Way app (endorsed by Ferriss) — Zen-based, 10–20 min/day — has direct evidence for anxiety reduction. Worth a trial.
Hype Watch: "Non-hallucinogenic psychedelics" are attracting significant VC capital, which biases the research toward positive outcomes. Ferriss correctly flags the Munger incentive problem: "Show me the incentive, and I will show you the outcome." Wait for independent replications before drawing clinical conclusions.
Wearable Zone 2 Calibration: A Real Protocol Problem
[Observational]

Given the 6-29% coefficient of variation in Zone 2 intensity markers documented in PMC research, most wearable-estimated Zone 2 training zones are systematically unreliable. Apple Watch, Garmin, and Whoop HR-based zone algorithms use population-average formulas that may be 10–20 BPM off your actual metabolic threshold.

Practical recalibration: use the nose-breathing test as a real-time Zone 2 ceiling. If you can breathe exclusively through your nose at a given pace, you're at or below VT1. The moment you need to open your mouth for a breath, you've crossed the threshold. No lab required. For HRV tracking on Whoop: Zone 2 sessions (when properly calibrated) should not suppress next-day HRV — if they do, you're training too hard.
ML-Personalised Sleep Intervention: Early Signal Worth Tracking
[Preprint]

A pilot RCT protocol (JMIR Research Protocols, 2026) is testing personalised machine-learning sleep interventions using consumer wearable data vs. generic sleep hygiene education. The study is establishing feasibility — results pending — but the design is notable because it uses existing consumer devices (rather than lab polysomnography) and personalises sleep prescriptions rather than applying population averages. Worth tracking when results land; it would validate or refute wearable-guided sleep optimisation at scale.

⚡ Stack change this week: Recalibrate your Zone 2 training zones using the nose-breathing test rather than HR-based formulas. If you're currently using a fixed HR ceiling, you may be training above your actual aerobic threshold and not getting the mitochondrial adaptation you're targeting.
Section 03 Fitness
ACSM 2026 Position Stand: The Volume-Centric View Wins — 137 Reviews, 30,000+ Participants
[Meta]

The American College of Sports Medicine published its first resistance training position stand update in 17 years on 17 March 2026, synthesising 137 systematic reviews covering 30,000+ participants. This is the most comprehensive meta-synthesis of resistance training science ever produced. The headline finding is clear and actionable: ≥10 sets per muscle group per week is the primary driver of hypertrophy, and this threshold holds across almost all training modalities — barbells, bands, bodyweight, machines.

The finding that load intensity (30–100% of 1RM) does not significantly differentiate hypertrophy outcomes when volume is equated is confirmed across multiple subgroup analyses. Training to momentary failure showed no meaningful advantage. Time under tension variations, exercise order, and specific periodisation strategies were all non-significant for hypertrophy outcomes when weekly volume was controlled. What was significant: eccentric-emphasised contractions produced superior hypertrophy vs. concentric-only approaches.

via ACSM Position Stand, PMC12965823

Jeff Nippard's Year-Long Beginner vs. Advanced Study: Measured at McMaster
[Observational]

Jeff Nippard released a year-long self-experiment comparing his own muscle growth (15+ years lifting experience) vs. his beginner brother on an identical program — every calorie tracked with MacroFactor, sleep monitored with Oura Ring, body composition measured via ultrasound, DEXA, BIA, and BioDex dynamometry at McMaster University. This is methodologically serious for YouTube science: actual lab instruments, not just visual comparison. Results are available in his latest upload. The study directly tests the beginner advantage hypothesis and provides real data on what a well-structured PPL produces in advanced lifters.

For dc specifically: if you're past the beginner stage, the takeaway is expectation calibration. Advanced lifters building meaningful muscle over a year should consider that a good outcome — not a failure. The ACSM guidelines confirm the path: ≥10 sets/week per muscle group, eccentric emphasis in key movements, progressive overload on compound lifts.
✓ What to Change
  • Audit weekly set counts per muscle group — confirm you're hitting ≥10 sets across the week for each major muscle
  • Add eccentric emphasis to 1–2 movements per muscle group (slow lowering phase, 3–4 sec)
  • Recalibrate Zone 2 using nose-breathing test, not HR formula
  • Daily moderate activity to sustain PEP/glycolytic flux for inflammation prevention
✗ What NOT to Change
  • Don't swap to heavier loads chasing hypertrophy — load range 30–100% 1RM all produce similar results when volume is equated
  • Don't restructure your program around periodisation schemes — when volume is controlled, periodisation doesn't significantly differentiate outcomes
  • Don't reduce creatine dosing — muscle/cognitive benefits are intact; just don't expect anti-inflammatory outcomes
⚡ Gym implication: Count your weekly sets per muscle group this week — the evidence is now unambiguous that hitting ≥10 is the single most important training variable for hypertrophy, and it's likely the first place PPL programs break down under real-world scheduling.

Section 05 Philosophy

Source: Daily Stoic — "Your Takeaways of the Week" · 28 March 2026

"But almost everything worthwhile — like wisdom, leadership, mastery, opportunity — takes far more time than we expect, than we want."

— Ryan Holiday / Daily Stoic · "It Takes Much Longer Than You Think (or Want)"

The Stoics called this amor fati — the love of fate — but it has a sharper sibling: the discipline of time patience. Marcus Aurelius spent the first forty years of his life in preparation before ascending to emperor; Epictetus taught for decades before his ideas were written down by a student. The Stoic framework is brutally clear that there is no shortcut between intention and mastery — and that the demand for immediacy is itself a form of vice, a corruption of judgement by anxiety.

For dc as a trader: the compounding of skill, read and pattern recognition in markets is no different. The Wyckoff methodology is not a signal-and-execute system — it's a perceptual discipline that deepens through repeated exposure to cycles. Every impatient trade — sized too large, taken too early, closed too soon — is a tax on the compounding of that perception. The market doesn't care about your timeline.

Weekly reading from Ryan Holiday: "It is always better to admire the best among our foes rather than the worst among our friends." — The Sympathizer by Viet Thanh Nguyen. Applied: judge the quality of ideas by the strongest version of the opposing argument, not its weakest.

Reflective question: What are you currently rushing — in training, in a trade, in a relationship — that would reward you more if you simply let it take the time it actually requires?
