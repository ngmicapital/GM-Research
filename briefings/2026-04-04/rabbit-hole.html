Rabbit Hole

The Man Who
Remembered It Wrong

Psychology 4 April 2026 · Sydney, Australia
TL;DR

In 1993, psychologist Daniel Kahneman discovered that people do not evaluate experiences the way their pain receptors do. Memory judges an episode by two moments only: its most intense point, and how it ended. Everything in between is largely ignored — a phenomenon called duration neglect. This means that a painful medical procedure lasting longer, and causing objectively more total pain, can be remembered as less bad than a shorter one — simply because it ended at a slightly less agonising moment. A 2003 randomised trial of 682 colonoscopy patients proved this in the real world, and showed a 41% increase in follow-up screening compliance as a result. The rule applies to pleasant experiences too, to exercise, to vacations, to entire lives. And Kahneman — who discovered it — died in March 2024 by assisted suicide in Switzerland, at 90, consciously engineering his own ending rather than letting decline write the final chapter.

Section 01

The Story

In 1993, Daniel Kahneman — then at Princeton, not yet a Nobel laureate — ran an experiment involving a tub of very cold water. Participants submerged one hand for 60 seconds at 14°C, which is painful without being medically dangerous: roughly the temperature of a cold mountain stream. Then, in a second trial, they submerged the other hand for 60 seconds at the same 14°C — and then kept it there for a further 30 seconds, during which the water was slowly warmed to 15°C. Still painful; measurably less so at the end.

After both trials, participants were told there would be a third trial and asked to choose which condition to repeat. The longer trial objectively involved more total pain: the same 60 seconds of 14°C, plus an additional 30 seconds of slightly warmer — but still unpleasant — water. Rationally, no one should prefer it. A significant majority did. They chose more suffering because the experience had a better ending.

Kahneman and his co-authors — Barbara Fredrickson, Charles Schreiber, and the Canadian physician Donald Redelmeier — had just documented the peak-end rule. They published it simultaneously in two papers in 1993, one establishing the effect itself (Psychological Science, 4(6)) and one naming its counterpart: duration neglect (Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 65(1)). The rule had three components. Memory evaluated experiences using the average of the peak intensity (the worst or best moment) and the end (the final impression). And duration — how long something actually lasted — contributed almost nothing to the memory.

Three years later, Redelmeier and Kahneman took the rule into a real hospital. They attached continuous pain-rating devices to patients undergoing colonoscopy and lithotripsy — kidney-stone fragmentation via sound waves — and had patients record their discomfort every 60 seconds throughout the procedure. Afterward, they asked for an overall global rating of "total pain." The correlation was striking: what predicted the remembered pain was not the average rating, not the duration, but the peak intensity and the pain during the final three minutes. A 20-minute colonoscopy that ended badly was remembered as worse than a 45-minute one that eased off at the close. The paper appeared in Pain in 1996.

That was the observation. The intervention came in 2003. Redelmeier, now with colleague Joel Katz, ran a randomised controlled trial on 682 consecutive outpatients undergoing colonoscopy. Half received the standard procedure. Half received the standard procedure plus an extended period — the colonoscope left stationary in the rectum briefly at the end, creating mild residual discomfort rather than the sharper pain of active movement. The two groups were then followed for a median of 5.3 years.

The extended group reported the experience as less unpleasant overall, despite having experienced more total procedure time and more total pain. On a 10cm visual analogue scale, they rated the experience 4.4 versus 4.9 for the control group (p = 0.006). More consequentially: the extended group was 41% more likely to return for repeat colonoscopy screening in the following years (odds ratio 1.41, p = 0.038). Colorectal cancer is highly treatable when caught early, and the primary barrier to catching it early is non-compliance with screening. Kahneman's cold-water experiment had become, five years later, a cancer-prevention intervention.

The Counterintuitive Core

Making a painful procedure longer — causing more total harm — improved its memory and increased the patient's future health compliance. This is not what ethics textbooks, medical consent forms, or pain-minimisation protocols would predict. It is also, demonstrably, true.

Section 02

The Mechanism

Kahneman spent years unpacking why the rule worked, and his explanation centred on a distinction he made famous in his 2011 book Thinking, Fast and Slow and his 2010 TED Talk: the difference between the experiencing self and the remembering self. These are not metaphors. They are two genuinely different systems processing the same life events, with different priorities and different data access — and they often want incompatible things.

The experiencing self lives in the present. Each conscious moment lasts roughly three seconds. It registers pain, pleasure, warmth, hunger — and then that moment is gone, leaving at most a faint residue in short-term memory before it vanishes. The experiencing self is the one who actually suffers through a colonoscopy or feels the joy of a sunset. It has no opinion about the past or the future. It only knows now.

The remembering self keeps score. It maintains the story of a life: what happened, how bad it was, whether it was worth it, whether to do it again. When you ask someone "how was your holiday?" or "how painful was that procedure?", it is the remembering self that answers. And the remembering self does not have access to a continuous pain log. It reconstructs based on a small number of memorable moments — disproportionately weighted toward intensity and endings.

In Plain English

Your experiencing self endures the pain. Your remembering self decides whether to go back. They use different information. Kahneman's key insight: "We don't choose between experiences — we choose between memories of experiences." Every decision about a future experience is made by a remembering self consulting an archive it has selectively edited.

Why endings? Kahneman and Fredrickson's work points to a feature of how episodic memory encodes events: the brain flags transitions — moments when affective state changes significantly — and uses the state at the boundary of an episode (its close) as a summary of what came before. Ending on a lower-pain note after high pain signals "it's over and it got better," which registers as the conclusion of the episode. Beginning-to-middle variation is retrospectively compressed into the peak.

The meta-analytic evidence is now robust. A 2022 analysis by Liefooghe and colleagues published in Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes synthesised 174 effect sizes and found a peak-end correlation of r = 0.581 — a large effect — with duration effects essentially at zero. The rule is strongest for simple, brief, unidimensional experiences (a cold-water trial, a medical procedure, a short film clip). For complex, multi-episode experiences — a two-week vacation, a semester of college — the average emotional experience across the whole event starts to matter more, and the peak-end rule weakens. But for the category of experiences where it applies, it applies reliably.

The exercise science literature has found a specific application with practical implications. A 2016 study by Zenko, Ekkekakis, and Ariely (co-authored by behavioural economist Dan Ariely) assigned 46 adults to 15-minute cycling sessions with intensity either ramping up or ramping down. Those whose sessions ended at a lower intensity reported substantially better post-exercise pleasure, better-remembered pleasure, and better-forecasted pleasure up to seven days later. The slope of the ending accounted for 35–46% of the variance in remembered and anticipated enjoyment. If gym programmes ended with gradually decreasing intensity — a kind of physiological better-ending — the research suggests compliance would rise.

Section 03
The Connections

The peak-end rule sits inside a broader and uncomfortable problem: the experiencing self and the remembering self have different interests, and no one is clearly in charge of resolving the conflict between them. Modern medicine is built around the experiencing self — it aims to minimise pain in real time, to shorten procedures, to achieve the fastest recovery. The colonoscopy trial suggests this is sometimes working against itself: minimising procedural duration can produce a worse remembered experience and reduce future compliance. Disney Parks figured this out empirically before Kahneman named it. Mickey Mouse characters are stationed at park exits by deliberate design, so that children's last memory of the day is a warm, magical farewell rather than a tired trudge to the car. Fireworks are scheduled at park closing for the same reason — a peak engineered as an ending. Restaurants place birthday dessert surprises not at the midpoint of a meal but near its close. The hospitality industry had reverse-engineered the rule decades before it appeared in a psychology journal. What Kahneman did was explain why it works. The ethical dimension sharpens this into a genuine dilemma. Deliberately extending a medical procedure to create a better ending causes the patient objectively more pain. The patient cannot be told this is happening — disclosure would defeat the effect by shifting expectations. A 2025 paper in the Journal of Evaluation in Clinical Practice (Armitage) concludes that this application fails the non-maleficence principle and cannot be saved by informed consent, because the mechanism requires concealment. The experiencing self is being sacrificed for the remembering self's benefit — without the experiencing self's knowledge or agreement. Kahneman himself never entirely resolved this. His death on March 27, 2024 — he died by assisted suicide in Switzerland, at the Pegasos organisation in Nunningen, aged 90, a fact revealed publicly in early 2025 — was interpreted by many as the ultimate personal application of his own research. He had watched his wife, the cognitive psychologist Anne Treisman, die after years of vascular dementia. He had written: "I have believed since I was a teenager that the miseries and indignities of the last years of life are superfluous, and I am acting on that belief." A man who spent 30 years studying how endings define memories chose, when he could, to write his own.

Section 04

Further Down the Hole

Biography Kahneman and Tversky: The Friendship Behind Behavioural Economics

The peak-end rule was late Kahneman, working largely with Redelmeier. The earlier revolution — prospect theory, loss aversion, cognitive biases — came from a two-decade collaboration with Amos Tversky, who died of melanoma in 1996 at 59, four years before Kahneman received the Nobel Prize in Economics (2002, the first psychologist to do so). Michael Lewis wrote The Undoing Project (2016) about their partnership. Kahneman repeatedly described Tversky's non-sharing of the Nobel as the great injustice of his career.

Philosophy The Self That Suffers vs. The Self That Decides

Kahneman's two-self framework maps onto a classical philosophical debate about personal identity. Derek Parfit's Reasons and Persons (1984) argued that personal identity over time is not what matters morally — that our future selves are, in some meaningful sense, different people. If the experiencing self and the remembering self are genuinely distinct agents with conflicting interests, whose preferences should medical ethics serve? The colonoscopy dilemma is a practical version of Parfit's puzzle, with real consequences for consent, autonomy, and what it means to act in a patient's "best interests."

Behavioural Science The Focusing Illusion and Why We Mis-forecast Happiness

Adjacent to the peak-end rule is Kahneman's "focusing illusion" — summarised in his aphorism: "Nothing in life is as important as you think it is when you are thinking about it." People overestimate the hedonic impact of major life events (winning the lottery, becoming paraplegic, moving to California) because when asked to imagine the future state, they focus on the change and ignore everything else — the rest of life's texture — that continues unchanged. Both rules explain why human happiness forecasting is systematically, predictably wrong.

Healthcare Design Engineering Better Endings in Medical Practice

Beyond colonoscopy, the peak-end rule has been applied to chemotherapy infusion suite design (warm parting interactions from nurses), emergency department discharge protocols (a final calm, explanatory conversation rather than abrupt paperwork), and paediatric vaccination sequences (saving the least painful jab for last). A particularly studied domain is post-operative physiotherapy: sessions structured to end with exercises patients can perform successfully — rather than ones they fail — produce better attendance and better self-efficacy outcomes at follow-up.

UX & Product How Software Designers Exploit Peak-End Psychology

The rule is now explicit design doctrine in UX. Onboarding flows are engineered to culminate in a satisfying completion moment — the confetti animation, the "you're all set" screen — rather than ending on a form field. Customer service interactions are designed with scripted warm closings. Subscription cancellation flows deliberately end with a concession ("we'll pause your account") to leave the memory of the service neutral rather than adversarial — maximising the probability of re-subscription. Jon Yablonski's Laws of UX (O'Reilly, 2020) codifies the peak-end rule alongside Fitts's Law and Hick's Law as a foundational design constraint.
