Rabbit Hole

The Committee That
Invented Trauma

Psychology 2 April 2026 · Sydney, Australia
TL;DR

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder did not exist before 1980. Not because the suffering didn't exist — it had been destroying soldiers since the American Civil War — but because the diagnosis hadn't been invented yet. PTSD was created by a small committee, lobbied into existence by anti-war psychiatrists and Vietnam veterans, and simultaneously shaped by feminist psychologists arguing that rape survivors and combat veterans were experiencing the same thing. The moment the American Psychiatric Association published the diagnosis in its Diagnostic and Statistical Manual, something remarkable happened: millions of people who had been told they were weak, cowardly, or simply broken suddenly had a name for what they were experiencing. And having a name changed everything — including what they experienced.

Section 01

The Story

In December 1870, a Union Army physician named Jacob Mendes Da Costa published a paper in the American Journal of the Medical Sciences describing a strange condition he had treated in over 400 soldiers during the Civil War. They suffered heart palpitations, dizziness, breathlessness, chest pain, and disturbed sleep. He called it Irritable Heart — later nicknamed "Soldier's Heart." His framing was almost entirely physical. The men's hearts were damaged, he believed, by the exertions of military service. He did not consider that what might be wrong was not their hearts but what they had seen.

Fifty years later, a British army physician named Charles Myers watched soldiers returning from the Western Front unable to speak, unable to walk, unable to stop trembling — men who had never been physically wounded but who had watched their comrades obliterated beside them. In February 1915 he published an article in The Lancet coining a new term: shell shock. The name was useful and immediately controversial. "Shell shock" implied physical cause — the brain damaged by concussive force. But many of the men had never been near an exploding shell. Military authorities found the diagnosis threatening. If shell shock was real, what did that mean for the hundreds of men shot for cowardice? Over 300 British soldiers were executed during WWI for desertion or cowardice. Some were almost certainly suffering from what we would now recognize as PTSD.

After WWI the condition was quietly filed away. After WWII — where it reappeared as "combat fatigue," affecting roughly 40% of all US military discharges — it was filed away again. The 1968 edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-II) had no category for trauma-induced illness at all. Veterans presenting with nightmares, flashbacks, emotional numbness, and hypervigilance could be classified only as having an "adjustment reaction" — a label that implied the problem was with them, not with what had happened to them.

Then came Vietnam. And something different happened.

A Note on the DSM

The DSM — Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders — is the American Psychiatric Association's official catalogue of mental illnesses. It defines what counts as a diagnosable condition, which determines who gets treatment, who qualifies for disability benefits, who can raise a psychiatric defense in court, and what insurers will pay for. Being in the DSM is not a trivial matter. It is the difference between a condition existing, officially, and not existing at all.

In December 1970, a psychoanalyst named Chaim Shatan and a Yale psychiatrist named Robert Jay Lifton began running informal "rap groups" in New York City with veterans from Vietnam Veterans Against the War. These sessions were deliberately anti-hierarchical — veterans refused the usual patient/doctor dynamic, insisting on equal authority over the understanding of their own experience. What emerged from hundreds of hours of those conversations was a recognizable, consistent syndrome: intrusive memories that returned without warning, emotional shutdown, explosive anger, inability to trust, a feeling that the self who had come back from Vietnam was not the same person who had left.

On May 6, 1972, Shatan published an op-ed in The New York Times under the headline "Post-Vietnam Syndrome," describing what he called "impacted grief — suffering that could not be mourned or resolved." He wrote: "The post-Vietnam syndrome confronts us with the unconsummated grief of soldiers — impacted grief in which an encapsulated, never-ending past deprives the present of meaning." After the piece ran, he later recalled, "the telephone was jumping off the wall." Over the next several years, Shatan and Lifton mounted a systematic campaign to get the condition into the next edition of the DSM. They compiled records on over 700 veterans, presented at APA conferences, went on radio, and eventually formed a committee — the Vietnam Veterans Working Group — that fed directly into the DSM revision process.

By 1980, they had won. The third edition of the DSM — DSM-III — included a new diagnosis: Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder. The crucial conceptual shift was in where the cause was placed. Prior diagnoses had, implicitly or explicitly, located the problem inside the individual: a weak character, a fragile constitution, a pre-existing vulnerability. DSM-III placed the cause outside: in the traumatic event itself. A veteran was not broken because he was weak. He was broken because of what had been done to him.

Section 02

The Mechanism

The story of PTSD's creation would be significant enough if it were just about Vietnam veterans. But at almost exactly the same moment, a parallel campaign was arriving at the same destination from a completely different direction.

In 1974 — the same year that Shatan and Lifton were building their clinical case for the DSM committee — two researchers at Boston City Hospital published a study in the American Journal of Psychiatry. Ann Wolbert Burgess, a psychiatric nurse, and Lynda Lytle Holmstrom, a sociologist, had followed 92 women admitted to the emergency ward with a presenting complaint of rape. What they documented was a two-phase syndrome: acute disorganization (shock, fear, inability to function) followed by a prolonged reorganization period characterized by nightmares, flashbacks, hypervigilance, and emotional numbing. They named it Rape Trauma Syndrome.

The symptom clusters were, to a striking degree, the same as what Shatan was documenting in Vietnam veterans. Feminist psychologists made the argument explicit: the psychological aftermath of sexual violence was structurally identical to combat trauma. The only reason they had been treated as categorically different was that one happened to men in war zones and the other happened to women in homes and on streets. This was not a medical distinction. It was a political one.

Harvard psychiatrist Judith Lewis Herman pulled these threads together most systematically in her 1992 book Trauma and Recovery, arguing that violence is violence and trauma is trauma, regardless of setting. She also identified a problem with the DSM-III definition. The manual had defined the traumatic stressor as "outside the range of usual human experience." But as feminist psychologists pointed out, sexual abuse of women and girls was statistically commonplace — anything but unusual. Making unusualness a criterion for diagnosis effectively excluded the most common forms of trauma that women experienced. Herman later proposed the diagnosis of Complex PTSD to capture the effects of prolonged, repeated trauma — childhood abuse, domestic captivity — as distinct from the single-incident model that had dominated the Vietnam-era discussions.

How Naming Changes Experience

Philosopher Ian Hacking coined the term "looping effect" to describe what happens when a diagnostic category meets the people it describes. Once PTSD existed as a named condition with a known cause and symptom profile, people began to understand their own experience through that frame. A veteran who had been managing nameless shame and inexplicable rage now had a map: this is a medical condition, it has an external cause, it is not a verdict on my character. The map changed the territory. This is not a criticism — it is one of the most powerful things that language does.

The legal effects followed almost immediately. Within months of the DSM-III publication, defense attorneys in criminal trials began introducing PTSD testimony to explain their clients' behavior. The condition entered criminal courts as a basis for defenses of insanity, diminished capacity, and sentencing mitigation. For rape survivors, Rape Trauma Syndrome was introduced in court to explain why victims often did not immediately report assaults, appeared calm, or returned to their attackers — behaviors that juries might otherwise interpret as evidence the assault hadn't occurred.

The treatment effects were equally profound. Once PTSD had an official etiology — an external traumatic event producing identifiable neurological and psychological changes — research funding followed. Decades of work on what happens to the brain under extreme stress, the role of the amygdala and hippocampus in traumatic memory, the mechanics of the fight-or-flight response, and ultimately the development of structured treatments — Cognitive Processing Therapy, Prolonged Exposure, EMDR — all flowed downstream from the 1980 DSM entry. Before the diagnosis existed, there was no consistent framework for treating it.

Section 03
The Connections

The PTSD story is one of the clearest examples of what philosopher Derek Summerfield, writing in the British Medical Journal in 2001, called "the invention rather than discovery" of a psychiatric condition — and why that distinction matters less than it first appears. Summerfield argued, not without force, that PTSD is "an entity constructed as much from sociopolitical ideas as from psychiatric ones," pointing to how cleanly its creation mapped onto specific political moments: the anti-war movement, feminist advocacy, the disability benefits system. He was right about the politics. But what he underestimated is that all diagnostic categories are partly political constructions — the question is whether they are useful constructions. The suffering described by Chaim Shatan's veterans and Ann Burgess's rape survivors was real before 1980. The name gave that suffering a shape. And the shape made it treatable, litigable, fundable, and — perhaps most importantly — speakable. Once something can be named, it can be survived differently. The history of PTSD is ultimately a history of what it costs, politically and personally, to make invisible suffering visible — and who has to fight to make that happen.

Section 04

Further Down the Hole

History Shell Shock and the Soldiers Who Were Shot for It

Over 300 British soldiers were executed during WWI for cowardice or desertion — charges that, in many cases, almost certainly described men incapacitated by what we now call PTSD. British psychiatrist W.H.R. Rivers pioneered a compassionate talking cure at Craiglockhart War Hospital, treating shell-shocked officers including poets Wilfred Owen and Siegfried Sassoon. The British government formally pardoned the executed soldiers in 2006. The 90-year delay is itself a story about diagnosis, politics, and who gets to define the difference between illness and moral failure.

Philosophy Ian Hacking and the "Looping Effect" of Mental Illness

Canadian philosopher Ian Hacking argued that psychiatric categories are not neutral descriptions — they actively reshape what they describe. He called it the "looping effect": a diagnosis changes how people understand and experience their symptoms, which feeds back into how the diagnosis is understood and refined. Unlike natural kinds (gold is gold whether or not you call it gold), mental illness categories are "interactive kinds" that loop between the label and the labelled. PTSD is one of his clearest examples. His books The Social Construction of What? and Rewriting the Soul go deep.

Critique The Cross-Cultural Trauma Debate

Derek Summerfield's 2001 BMJ paper argued that PTSD is culturally specific — a Western, individualist framework for suffering that does not translate cleanly to societies with different meaning-making traditions. His critique of "global mental health imperialism" points to cases where Western trauma workers deployed after disasters in non-Western countries imposed diagnostic frameworks that disrupted local coping systems rather than supporting them. The debate remains unresolved: some trauma responses appear cross-culturally consistent; the meaning attached to them, and who counts as a patient, varies enormously.

Neuroscience What PTSD Actually Does to the Brain

Since the 1980 diagnosis opened the research floodgates, neuroscientists have mapped PTSD's neurological fingerprint: a hyperactive amygdala (the brain's threat-detection system) that fires in response to stimuli associated with the original trauma; a suppressed prefrontal cortex (which normally dampens emotional responses); and a disrupted hippocampus that encodes traumatic memories differently from ordinary ones — less as narrative and more as sensory fragments. This is why flashbacks feel like reliving rather than remembering. The body keeps the score, as Bessel van der Kolk's 2014 book of that name put it — and that score is written in neurophysiology.

Biography Judith Herman and the Politics of Trauma

Judith Herman spent her career at the intersection of psychiatry, feminism, and justice. Her 1992 book Trauma and Recovery — still the foundational text in the field — argued that the history of psychiatry's engagement with trauma has been a history of repeated forgetting: the field discovers the reality of traumatic suffering, documents it rigorously, and then, under social and political pressure, retreats into disbelief. The pattern had happened after WWI, after WWII, and, she argued, would happen again. Her call to remember — to hold the knowledge against the political tendency to suppress it — remains urgent.
